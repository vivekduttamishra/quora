The incident of Mahabharata dates back over 5000 years, but it's compilation in a written form is no more than 2000 years. For a long time the epic was propagated via great Indian tradition of Shruti and Smriti. Each generation heard and memorized the epic and passed on to the generation next. While some noise got added and some distortion did happen in the process, the core epic was mostly preserved which was not possible in any written media.

The very first version by Ved Vyas known as Jaya was merely 8800 shlokas. That doesn’t mean every thing above 8800 verses is an interpolation. It was a summary of entire incident. Ved Vyas later expanded on those incidents and the new compilation Bharata was more than 24000 verses which later expanded to Mahabharata as we know it today. Those interested in the detailed journey can follow my article here — The endless journey - the beginning and the milestones

Now coming to ancient written compilation, there is no single source of truth. Apart from Sanskrit, there are nepali, bengali, maithili, Sharda version which are collectively known as nothern versions. And then there are southern versions.

Now all these variations have common truth — how the evil of the era, Kauravas and Karna were destroyed by Pandavas representing good people and guided by Lord Krishna. But there are certain variations in the description of minor incidents. And to weed out such interpolations that —

  1. Incidents or references that existed in just few of the those many references, were marked as interpolations.
  2. Incidents that have no cross-references are removed.
  3. Incidents that are described differently in different editions are selected based on point 1 and 2.

Case Study 1 — Draupadi Rejected Karna in Swyamvara

This is one of the most popular narrative, at least in todays retelling. However, this incident was present in just 5 out of 1259 manuscripts that were available to BORI for analysis. It excluded southern recitations, the oldest avaialble nepali edition, bengali edition etc.

It was present in Neelkantha edition, a popular North Indian recitation and the basis of KMG version (the first English Edition). However the same edition has a contradiction to the incident and again there was no cross reference. Thus BORI ruled it out as an interpolation. Case Study 2 — Draupadi Vastraharan

The incident is present in all the ancient recitation without any exception. However the details have been slightly different. It has tons of cross-references to prove the incident occured. BORI retained this incident, with one change — They suggested Dharama, and not explicitly Krishna, protected Draupadi.

It must be noted that few contemporary critics try to rule out entire vastra-haran as an interpolation. However, it must be perceived as their perception, without any foundation. It must be noted that BORI has explicitly included vastraharan incident.

C O N C L U S I O N

  1. There is no single version that is called authentic.
  2. All versions don’t agree on the number of verses.
  3. When a critical edition is created with the goal of removing interpolation, it is natural that those verses will go.
  4. Verses that are removed were mostly present in few and not all recitation.
