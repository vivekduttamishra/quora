Why wasn't the act of a king marrying multiple women seen as an "Aadharm" act, but a woman (Daupadi) marrying multiple men (5 Pandavas) term as "aadharm"?

📅 Oct 13, 2020 08:18 PM PDT  |  🌐 English

The question is based on a flawed premise —

Polyandry (woman marrying multiple men) while an extremely rare occurrence, was not seen as Adharma.

Anything uncommon attracts scrutiny and people having no knowledge of Dharma, people who devoted entire life in conspiracy and evil act may be keen in calling others a sinner, and few may even toe their line, but…

Who called Draupadi’s marriage as Adharma?

Only one person in the entire epic. And if that person really had the knowledge of Dharma, would he have sided with Adharma all his life? For few people murder is diplomacy and a Vedic marriage a sin. What can we say?

For them tormenting people in misery is the parameter of self happiness, and a happy family reason to resent. What can we say again?

Naturally those who don't find stealing cettles from other kingdoms must be virtuous enough to be quoted as authority on Dharma.

Who approved the marriage?

Mahadeva Shiva, the God of Gods and the originator of Dharma, blessed Draupadi to marry five husband's. Blessed, not cursed. Surely he knew more Dharma than some hypocrite.

Vedvyas the author of several scriptures that defines conducts, approved of marriage.

Lord Krishna approved the marriage and marriage was conducted under vedic rituals.

There is no reference that public talked against the marriage or considered it Adharma.

There were few, but still existing reference to polyandry.

Outside Mahabharata

Both polygamy (many wife) and polyandry exists outside Mahabharata even till date. Both are on decline and polyandry is rarer. But exists.

You must note polyandry is an English word with non Mahabharata origin and not created by Vedvyas.

Hope that answers the question.
