WHAT WAS THE ETHICS OF USING DIVYASTRA (DIVINE WEAPONS)

While nothing is explictly mentioned for Bhargavastra, there was a general protocol for the use of any Divyastra. Here are few important references.

1. Drona to Arjuna while providing Brahmsira

Bharadvaja’s son told the great-souled maharatha, “O mighty-armed one! Receive this invincible and supreme weapon, named brahmashira, with the knowledge of releasing it and withdrawing it. You must never use it against human beings. If it is used against an enemy whose energy is inferior, it will burn up the entire universe.

—Debroy, Bibek. Mahabharata Vol. 1 (Kindle Locations 5106-5108).

2. Parshurama’s warning to Karna

However, he25 smiled and said, ‘You acted in this false way because of your greed for weapons. O stupid one! In a different place, when the time for your death has come, you will be engaged in a fight with someone who is your equal and the brahmastra will not manifest itself before you. The qualities of a brahmana will never remain with someone who is not a brahmana. Leave this place, since this is not meant for an untruthful one like you.

—Debroy, Bibek. Mahabharata: Volume 8 (Kindle Locations 2403-2406).

3. Shiva’s advise to Arjuna while providing Pashpata

Partha! But it must not be suddenly released at any man. If it is released at someone who lacks in energy, it will destroy the entire universe. There is no one in the three worlds, mobile or immobile, who cannot be killed by it.

—Debroy, Bibek. Mahabharata Vol. 2 (Kindle Locations 5484-5486).

4. Indra’s warning to Karna while providing Vasvishakti

O Karna! You will again possess the complexion and energy of your father. Your complexion will again become like his. But if you unleash this invincible weapon in a fit of fury, when you possess other weapons, there is no doubt that it will descend on you.”

—Debroy, Bibek. Mahabharata Vol. 3 (Kindle Locations 8560-8562).

WHAT IS THE CORE COMBINED WISDOM?

  • You shouldn’t use the divine weapon recklessly
  • You should never use these weapons on someone weaker than you.
  • You should use these weapons only as the last resort only.

HOW DO YOU COUNTER A DIVINE WEAPON?

A divyastra could be countered by higher Divyastras, but as we read in our epic, it is always preferential not to counter those weapons with anything, lest, you may end up doing more damage.

  1. Ashwatthama invokes Narayanastra. It was a mighty weapon. How was it neutralized? On Krishna’s suggestions, Pandavas surrendered and Naryanastra finding no enemy retreated.
  2. Bhagdatta invoked Vaishnavastra. Did Krishna countered it with his sudarshan chakra? No He simply stood in front of it an absorved it.
  3. Ashwatthama invoked Brahmastra. Arjuna, too, invoked Brahmastra but finally retracted it as the collision could have dire consequences.

WHAT HAPPENED WITH BHARGAVASTRA?

Karna used the Bhargavastra against Pandavas Army. Now this invocation was a violation to the ethics of using Divyastra, unless of course he thought those foot soldiers better than himself. When a weapon is fired recklessly, it has adverse consequences.

WHY DID ARJUNA FLEE?

Here is the most loved text of Karna fans in whole of Mahabharata —

‘ “At this, Kunti’s son, Dhananjaya, spoke to Vasudeva. ‘Behold the extremely terrible bhargava weapon. O Krishna! O mighty-armed one! Behold the valour of the bhargava weapon. There is no means of countering that weapon in a battle. O Krishna! Behold the angry son of a suta in the great battle. The brave one is like Yama and has performed a terrible deed. He is repeatedly casting extremely terrible glances towards me. I do not see any means of running away from Karna in this battle.

—Debroy, Bibek. Mahabharata: Volume 7 (Kindle Locations 3599-3602).

Should we take Arjuna’s word literally? Was there really no means to counter the weapon? Let me give you a hint. Arjuna also says there is no means of running away from Karna in this battle. But people claim he ran away. Isn’t that a paradox?

So what did Arjuna really mean when he said there is no means of running away? He meant running away isn’t an option. What did he mean when he said there is no means of countering that weapon? He meant the obvious means to destroy it with higher weapon is not an option — Arjuna would need to stoop down to Karna’s level or recklessness.

What does Ved Vyas say, why Arjuna left the battle?

Here is Ved Vyas’s interpretation —

Krishna told Arjuna, ‘The king561 has been severely wounded. O best among the Kuru lineage! Let us reassure him first and you will kill Karna after that.’ Dhananjaya also wished to see the king who had been afflicted with arrows. On Keshava’s instructions, he abandoned the battle and quickly departed on his chariot. Kounteya left, because he wished to see Dharmaraja. Though he looked at all the soldiers, he could not see his elder brother anywhere. O descendant of the Bharata lineage! Having fought with Drona’s son and having defeated the descendant of Bhrigu lineage,562 whom even the wielder of the vajra found difficult to withstand in battle, he departed. He defeated Drona’s son, the wielder of a fierce bow. He thus accomplished an extremely difficult deed.

—Debroy, Bibek. Mahabharata: Volume 7 (Kindle Locations 3605-3610).

Note:

  • The King Yudhisthir was severly wounded and wasn’t seen anywhere. It was very important for Arjuna to go on looking for him. His safety was paramount.
  • Arjuna left, because he wished to see Dharmaraja, not because he was afraid (Ved Vyas’s word. Not mine)
  • He defeated Ashwatthama on the way and Ved Vyas calls it an extremely diffcult deed. Tell me how many times a fleeing Karna defeated anyone? He certainly holds the record of fleeing most of the time in the battlefield.

MOST IMPORTANT — WHY DIDN’T KRISHNA ASKED ARJUNA TO COUNTER BHARGAVASTRA?

As I started, never interrupt your enemies when they are making a mistake. Karna had committed a blunder of using the Divyastra against common soldiers, and Krishna had seen through it —

Janardana wished to leave to see Yudhishthira and thought that Karna would be overcome with exhaustion in the battle.560

—Debroy, Bibek. Mahabharata: Volume 7 (Kindle Locations 3603-3604).

Now this is an interesting thought —

  • Karna has fired the Divyastra.
  • No one is fighting Karna.
  • Krishna still thinks — Karna would be overcome with exhaustion in the battle!

What do you make out of this observation. I am sure if you read though the entire answer, you would solve this riddle — Karna had misused the Divyastra and Krishna knew it would backfire, making Karna tired.

Was Krishna right in his assumption? Ask yourself few more questions —

  • How many Maharatahas were slain by this weapon? None.
  • Why, when no one encountered it, the weapon didn’t destroy entire Pandavas army bringing victory to Duryodhana?

Now ask the most important question — What would have happened, had Arjuna countered this weapon?

If Arjuna had countered this weapon, Karna’s action would had been justified — it would be fired against a superior warrior. It would have done lot more damage to Pandavas than to Karna. The master strategist saw though the ultimate irony — fight and you suffer; ignore and you win!

Krishna drove away Arjuna to two important missions —

  1. Search Yudhistir and assure him, Karna would be killed. That happened.
  2. Let Karna get tired of his own initiation and reduce the damage. Bhargavastra killed thousands of food soldiers, but failed to boost any prosptect for Karna or Kauravas.
