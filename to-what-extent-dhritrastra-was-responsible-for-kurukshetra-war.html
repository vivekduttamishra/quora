To what extent Dhritrastra was responsible for Kurukshetra war?

📅 Apr 21, 2020 09:46 AM PDT  |  🌐 English

Thanks for A2A.

If only one person who can be held for responsible for Kurukshetra war, he is Dhritrashtra.

  1. Born blind with an ambition to become King, he never let his ambition go. He constantly blamed everyone for it. It blinded his inner self.
  2. His ambition was fulfilled soon, when his younger brother Pandu abdicated the throne. This was high time he should have been contained. Alas!
  3. Dhritrashtra was coronated but did he never really became the king? Did he ever cared about the kingdom? Did he ever thought beyond his own ambition?
  4. His ambition and lust for throne extended beyond his self. Now he wanted his son to succeed. His younger brother had transferred kingship to him. What did he do in return? He let the rift grow between his and his brothers son.
  5. He was not just aware of the Varnavrat conspiracy, he was a party to it. He pushed Not just Pandavas but even Kunti to what he thought was a sure death.
  6. He preferred to partition his kingdom rather than chosing the rightful heir. This was his responsibility as the king.
  7. Then came the great gamble and chirharan episode. Everyoone knows what happened? Who should we blamed? Shakuni for deceit, or Duryodhana for ordering Draupadi to be brought as slave, or Dussasan for carryng his bidding or Karna for asking Dussasan to disrobe her? Franking none of them is more responsible than the king in whose chamber the incident unfolded
  8. Afraid of Draupadi’s curse he returned him everything only to force them to gamble again and again forgetting all his duty as the king, he exiled them.
  9. Finally Krishna offered Hastinapur a peace deal which was rejected. Who rejected them? Duryodhana? Think again. Duryodhana was not the king. It can be understood, that by now Dhritrashtra was too helpless against the ambition that Duryodhana had grown of his own, and Dhritrashtra was more like Manmohan Singh by now. But then he was a sworn King and responsible for everything that transpired under his command.
  10. He let his army vanish and continued to hear the commentry from Sanjay, realizing his mistake but never really trying to stop the battle.
