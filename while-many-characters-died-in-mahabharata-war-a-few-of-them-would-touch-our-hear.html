While many characters died in Mahabharata war a few of them would touch our heart as to the manner how they died finally. Can you rank them as per your preferences from most tragic being the rank one? And explain why you feel so?

📅 Jun 21, 2022 02:13 AM PDT  |  🌐 English

Thanks for the A2A.

This question calls for personal opinion. Therefore before moving forward to understand tragic death we must underline that deaths and devastations or a useful life cut short should be considered a tragedy.

With enough preface, here is my list of tragic deaths —

6.BHURISRAVA

Bhurisrava was a strong fighter. He defeated Satyaki. He may have tried to kill him unethically, but nevertheless defeated him fairly. Arjuna punished him for his misdeed and severed his hand. He did repent for his fault. And then, the next moment, he was dead. Killed when he least expected it. He had paid his debt by losing his hand and he certainly didn’t deserve the death he got. But the biggest tragedy was the fact that Satyaki didn’t get the reprimand he deserved. Bhurisrava’s death remained unjustified.

5.BROTHERS OF KARNA

Two brothers of Karna died in different battles. Neither of them were killed unfairly. First was killed by Arjuna during the Matasya battle and the second was slayed by Abhimanyu on the thirteenth day of the Kurukshetra battle. So how was their death a tragic? The tragedy was, the fact that they were abandoned by one person, they thought would be their protector. The tragedy was, there was no one to stand for them while they died standing for their worthless evil brother for whom his own sorry life had the highest priority.

4.SON OF KARNA

Why should the death of Karna’s son be separated from the death of Karna’s brothers. Because his was more tragic than others. He death was a live proof of the phrase ‘sins of father’. His father’s abusive actions earned him a nemesis — the greatest warrior of the era. His father sealed his fate. And I must say, he died a hero’s death. The tragedy was there was no one to protect him.

3.JAIDRADH

Jaidradh’s death is one of the most tragic death. He was just a small lever in a big machinery and had not done anything unfair. He had a boon that made him invanquishable. He used it against his enemy in favour of his in laws.

But then he was betrayed. Everyone knew well that he was good as dead, if Arjuna has taken an oath to kill him. Yet, everyone gave him a false sense of security that they would protect him. Jaidradh paid the price of trusting them who had repeatedly proved that they were not worth trusting. He trusted back-stabbers and paid the price. I find that tragic.

2.THE VICTIMS OF ASHWATTHAMA, THE NIGHT HUNTER

A group of victors were sleeping peacefully in their tent. They had fought a war against warriors and had won. Their life was cut short. The tragedy was, they didn’t get the death of hero that they were. They were denied the opportunity. The tragedy was a great warrior, son of the greatest acharya of the time, chose to abandon his father’s teaching and chose and owl as his guru.

1. ASHWATTHAMA

Yes, perhaps that is the greatest tragedy is the death of Ashwatthama. Yes, you may be wondering, nay doubting, at this point if I know anything about Mahabharata if I am talking about the death of Ashwatthama. But trust me on this. The warrior Ashwatthama died with his father. The hunter Ashwatthama wouldn’t die anytime soon. The greatest tragedy was waiting for the end of the life that was a life long punishment for moment of passion and crime. Blessed were those who died earlier.

WHEN DEATH WAS NOT A TRAGEDY

Perhaps many would disagree with my choice of ruling out Abhimanyu’s death as a tragedy. Abhimanyu was a hero and the death of a hero in the line of duty can’t be a tragedy. It is something to be celebrated and cherished. Abhimanyu defeated an entire army. Alone. He unmasked the cowards who fancied themselves as warriors and spoilt their conspiracy and in dying unleashed the wrath of his father that decisively changed the course of the war. Few can claim to attain such a success as he did. Death of such a warrior can’t be marked as tragedy.

Similary, death (or removal) of Bhishma was that of a hero. He existed out his own will, sharing his secret and embracing his end as a true friend. His greatness can be asserted by the fact that Krishna himself got up to kill him. Not once, but twice. For others, he didn’t need to lift even his finger. How many other can even dream of meeting their death at their own terms? Can we call it a tragedy?

Now contrast these heroic deaths with that of Karna and Duryodhana. They were so terrified of dying that one hid inside a lake while other desperately looked for ways to flee in every battle and asked for a recesses when his escape was obstructed by a chariot with wheel stuck in the mud of his crimes. And more important, the war was their creation. Everyone who died was because of them. We have a tradition of celebrating the death of such evils. We don’t call them tragedy. We call it victory of good over the evil —
