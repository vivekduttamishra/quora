It is not just a hypothetical situation for me — If given a chance to write the biography. My over three years' effort is now an Amazon#1 best-seler novel.

Whose biography did I write?

I would love to call it the autobiography of The Lost Epic — Mahabharata. In past several decades, I feel, the epic has been brutalized, again and again. TV Shows, character specific perceptions, they have done a great dis-service to the epic in the name of the creativity. Facts are belittled, fabrications are added, and they lose the original spirit of the epic.

Stories have often been woven around a particular character and to glorify the character, the epic itself was shunted out. Some one would potray Karna-Draupadi love story, others would make Shakuni a mastermind avenging the fictious death of his family and yet another would potray Arjuna in such a delusion, that he mistook Eklavya for Shiva!

Born in India, the land of story-tellers, I am not against creative edges in the story and don’t find adding new chapters to the epic a sin. It has been done again and again. It is the intention that we should really be worried about — Is the purpose to bring out the greatness of the epic or ridicule it?

Let me tell you two short stories to bring out my points —

1.Lakshman Rekha

Those of you who have actually read original Balmiki Ramayana would know, it doesn’t have any reference of Lakshman Rekha. Even Ramayana by Ved Vyas as part of Mahabharata doesn’t contain any reference. Even Ramcharit Manas, the most popular recitation in north India has only passing by reference to Lakshman Rekha.

Yet I believe, Lakshman Rekha has an excellent reason to be present and I endorse such kind of add-ons. It doesn’t aim at distorting the epic and introduces a beautiful message. If you check it out carefully, you would recognize Lakshman Rekha became part of Ramayana that came around and after Mugal envasion in Indian (1000AD). Loot, adbduction and rape was on the rise. And it was a subtle message to keep the family togehter and women safe, in constant company of the family member. Even today, it is a proverb for maintaining the protocol.

There are uncountable such small and beautify stories, which enriches the epic without being dishonest to it. In this case Lakshman was concerned about the safety and well being of Sita and drawing a line is adding details not altering it or defacing it.

2.Drona refuses to Teach Karna

This is another popular story. Drona refused to teach Karna because he was a Suta — a popular story to showcase how rigid and exploiting our ancient society was. But the problem is this fiction is against the basic spirit of the epic.

Drona never refused to teach Karna. And this is a great proof that guru’s didn’t turn down a student because of caste. They may reject someone for a different reason. And this coincides with Ramayana where Rama and Nishada were classmates.

When someone writes such a fabricated story, it not only distorts the epic but also presents a distorted perspective about our ancient culture, society and the great nation we are.

What is the difference between these two stories?

First story (Lakshman Rekha) is a constructive add-on to the epic trying to use epic as a platform to propagate a useful and protective message without being deceitful to the epic.

Second story is a character centric (Karna) story. When you write a biography for a single character and expect to hightlight it, you need to censure the orginal epic to suit your narrative. You not only potray Drona in a bad light, but the entire era in a bad light. If you want to proof a villain a hero, you would need to sling mud on the heros and potray them as villain. And when the mud slinging starts, no one escapes — Krishna, Shiva, Ved Vyas even the epic. And that is where we have The Lost Epic.

So, whose biography did I write?

It is a story that tries to trace the origin of Mahabharata with one important question — Why was it a Dharmayudha? Can a family fued for a throne be called Dharmayudha?
