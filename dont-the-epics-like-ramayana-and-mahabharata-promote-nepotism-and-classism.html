Don't the epics like Ramayana and Mahabharata promote nepotism and classism?

📅 Jul 20, 2020 10:13 PM PDT  |  🌐 English

This is an interesting question — trying to push in nepotism and classism in our epic.

Before we proceed with this new and trending word to attack our epic, let me ask you a few simple questions —

  • Your father’s property should go to you or distributed in society? Is this a matter of heredity or nepotism? What does the law say?
  • Can we consider a mother feeding her children, when there are thousands of starving children, nepotism?
  • You eat lunch in a lavish restaurant with your friends and family but don’t invite a stranger to your table, nepotism?
  • When I wrote my novel, I trusted my friends and family to read its draft, as I feared that if I give my manuscript to an outsider, it may be stolen, Is it nepotism?

In an eagerness to use this newfound shiny word on our epic, one has missed the basic definition of Nepotism. So let us begin by understanding Nepotism —

The term comes from Italian word nepotismo, which is based on Latin root nepos meaning nephew. Since the Middle Agest and until the late 17th century, some Catholic popes and bishops, who had taken vows of chasity, and therefore usually had no legitimate offspring of their own, gave their nephews such positions of preference as were often accorded by fathers to son. Several popes elevated nephews and other relatives to the cardinalate. Often, such appointments were a means of continuing a papal "dynasty"

We must understand that the word nepotism can’t apply to a dynasty or heredity which is a legal right.

In Mahabharata and Ramayana, when one sees superficially, it was a dynasty and not nepotism. So you can blame them, till the time you read the next section, for being dynast or promoting classism but not with the word Nepotism.

Dynasty, Class and the law of nature

We must understand that, when Krishna said he created the varnas, he was talking about the law of nature. Hierarchy is the law of nature and doesn’t apply only to human population —

  • In a bee hive, there is a queen bee.
  • Tigers and Deers can’t change their respective places, Nepotism or not.
  • We have a natural tendency to classify —
    • Reptiles and Mammals
    • Carnivore and Herbivore
    • Chairman, CEO, VP, Manager and worker

Sanatan Culture and Class

Krishna says he created the varnas —

  • Brahmin from the head (brain)
    • Thinkers, scientiest, legislatures
    • Those who setup the infrastructure
  • Kshatriya from the breast
    • indicates the courage
    • protector of the society
  • Vaisya from the stomac
    • Feeder of the society
    • Earns the money by trade etc.
  • Shudra from the legs
    • Manual labour
    • Service industry

Krishna compares each of this varna symbolically with the body limbs not only as metaphor but also to highlight each of them are integral and important part of the society and it can’t function either without header or leg or anything in between. How many of us consider our leg as second-class citizen?

The problem started when these varnas were made hierarchial — high and low and, I must say, it started very late in India after a series of invasions my mangols, mugals and Britishers. They divided the society using our older system and converting them to their hierarchy.

Ancient Indian varnas were a lot better than —

  • Modern organization structure —
    • where there is huge pay and perk difference between senior and junior management. Why no one blames them for classism?
    • In our ancient India, Brahmins like Drona were devoid of properties and Adhiratha, the foster father of Karna, was an affluent man.
  • Slave and Racism as were prevelent on many parts of the globe —
    • In ancient epic system, there isn’t much concept slavery seen, where ever mentioned is done with a frown.
    • The maids of kings and queens were well to do and treated with great care — you may consider the example of Manthara or Trijata from Ramayana and Shialendary (Draupadi) from Mahabharata.

Ancient India and Nepotism

Let us see, how rigid was the classism or favourism in the ancient Indian society —

  • King Bharata side steps his sons and selects and outsider as his successor.
  • Both in Ramayana and Mahabahrata, princes study in an ashram with other kids without any preferential treatment —
    • Rama studies with Nishada and other children.
    • Pandavas studies with Karna in Drona’s gurukul.
    • Krishna, a prince, studies with Sudama and both were entrusted with the same duties, together.
  • Drona gives the full knowledge of Brahmastra to Arjuna, but doesn’t give the same to his own son Ashwatthama — Nepotism?

There are almost negligible instances of nepotism in our epic, unless you consider Duryodhana crowning his friend Karna as a King. But this was the action of an ambitious Prince to gain Karna’s favour, so I am not sure if it fits perfectly in the definition of Nepotism. It was more of a mutually beneficial relationship.

My answer is based on the story of Mahabharat, the Great Indian Epic.
