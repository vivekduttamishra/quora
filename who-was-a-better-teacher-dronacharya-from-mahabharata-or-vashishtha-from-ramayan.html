Who was a better teacher, Dronacharya from Mahabharata or Vashishtha from Ramayana?

📅 Apr 24, 2020 11:31 PM PDT  |  🌐 English

This question calls for speculation. And while we can make a brave attempt to compare the two well renowned guru’s of great epic heros, we must consider one important point which we often miss in comparisons like this —

Vashishtha and Dronacharya were not contemporary, not even close. They represent two different yuga — Treta and Dwaper. The society changed considerably during these two periods. Their situations were different which resulted in them taking different actions. So if we search for an ideal teacher without taking into account one of the most vital variable, that is the era, the conclusion can’t be accurate.

Vashishtha

Is one of the most renowned of guru’s, with his span starting right from Satyug and extending unto early parts of Dwaper. He attained the status of Brahmrishi, the highest among the rishis. His desciples included not only Bhisma and Great Ram, but also others from the Ikshavaku clans like — Harishchandra and Bhagiratha.

Vashishta followed a strict gurukul rule, with no interference from Kings. He raised a worthy disciple and worthy sons. There goes a story in Balkanda of Balmiki Ramayana —

The King Satyavrata, the father of Harishchandra, wanted to ascend to heaven with his physical body. He was a virtuous King. But Vashishtha refused to accommodate his demands against Vedas. Then he approached Vashishtha’s son Shakti and lured him to do his bidding. Shakti realizing that the King is asking him to go against his guru and father cursed him. He became Trishanku. Rest is a different story.

— Story referred from Balkanda of Balmiki Ramayna

Note a few points —

  1. Vashishtha cursed the King for breaking the rules of shashtras.
  2. The Guru’s Son respects his guru and father more than his personal gains.

Dronacharya

Dronacharya was no Brahmrishi but a teacher of Dwaper yug. His achievement included almost all the great warriors from Kauravas, Pandavas, Vrishinis, Andhaks and not excluding the great archer Karna and great warrior Arjuna. It speaks volume about him.

But unlike Vashishtha or his son, Dronacharya and Ashwatthama became obidient to his benefector King. He became the guru by exhibiting his skills to impress Bhisma and fought the King’s battle in Kurukshetra. His son was ready to do the bidding of friends ignroing his father’s wishes.

Let us go a few years back. His friend from Gurukul, King Drupada, insulted Drona. In retaliation, he vanquishes Drupada by the hand of his desciples. It was the Gurudakshina. A guru asks his disciple to vanquish his enemy?

So who is a better Guru? Are we missing the variable?

Why did Drona reached out to his friend Drupada in the first place?

Because he was struggling with extreme poverty with his son yarning for a cup of milk? Do we see such a reference in Treta or Satyuga. Brahmins always relied on alms. So what happened in Dupara? That source of Alms dried up to such an extent that a teacher couldn’t afford a glass of milk. How would a guru teach his disciple the science of protection if he is struggling with basic chorus of life?

In treta a guru was above the king. A king would visit the guru and guru can refuse his desciple his unjust demands. But in Dwapar, a king insults not just any Brahmin but his friend. This represets a disblance in social structure. Drona vanquishes his ego. He doesn’t ask Hastinapur’s help, but his disciples. His disciples represents his own teaching power.

Both rejected unworthy demands by their disciple. Drona may appear subdued in front of Vashishtha, more compromising. But I believe it is the variable that should be accounted.

Another variable we must consider while reading the answer is realization that neither belonged to our current era. And that too matters.

My answer is based on the story of Mahabharat and Ramayan, the Great Indian Epics.
