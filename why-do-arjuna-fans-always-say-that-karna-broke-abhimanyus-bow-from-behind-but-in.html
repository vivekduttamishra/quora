Why do Arjuna fans always say that Karna broke Abhimanyu’s bow from behind, but in reality Arjun cut Bhurishrava's hand from behind?

📅 Feb 06, 2021 11:16 PM PST  |  🌐 English

Thanks for the A2A.

I assume questioned, here accepts Karna broke Abhimanyu’s bow from behind and the only focus of the question is to say — Even Arjuna did the same.

But the question is, does Arjuna’s action justifies that of Karna? And even more important question: are they even comparable?

Let us first put the perspective of comparison right. Back here is not literally back. Hitting from behind implies application of unethical means, something that a warrior shouldn’t deploy and should be censored. So let us compare the two incident and try to understand the opinion of those people who can be considered unbiased and trustworthy for the context.

Abhimanyu was killed

When Abhimanyu was killed here was what Sanjay said to Dhritrashtra —

‘“O king! Thus, in that battle, a single one was slain by many. He had agitated all the soldiers, like an elephant among lotuses. The brave one was as resplendent as a wild elephant killed by hunters. The brave one fell down and was surrounded by all those on your side.

—Debroy, Bibek. Mahabharata Vol. 6 (Kindle Locations 891-893). Penguin Books Ltd. Kindle Edition.

  • This statement clearly thrawts any claim that Abhimanyu was killed by son of Dussassana in a fair duel.
  • True, Dussassana was the last one to fight Abhimanyu. But by then he was battle bitten, mutilated and tired and was devoid of any support.
  • Karna was part of those many who killed Abhimanyu. He was the one who cut down his bow, when the later was not fighting him but someone else.

ARJUNA STRIKES BHURISRAVA

Instead of giving my opinion or someone elses opinion, let Bhurisrava answer for himself —

Thus addressed by Partha, he400 (Bhurisrava) touched the ground with his head. With his left arm, he offered his severed right arm. O great king! Having heard Partha’s words, the immensely radiant one, with the sacrificial altar on his standard, remained silent and hung his head down.

—Debroy, Bibek. Mahabharata Vol. 6 (Penguin Translated Texts) (Kindle Locations 4398-4400). Penguin Books Ltd. Kindle Edition.

Why, if Arjuna was wrong, Bhurisrava remained silent and hung his head down? What did Arjuna really said? Well, he said two things —

  1. It was Bhurisrava who was attacking an unconcious unarmed warrior and trying to kill him when the later couldn’t fight. That was Adharma. Arjuna stopped him in his henious act.
  2. Arjuna reminded him the way they had killed the lone warrior Abhimanyu. Remember Abhimanyu, Bhurisrava bowed his head is shame. This once again is a cross-reference to Abhimanyu’s unjustified killing.
  3. Once Bhurisrava recognizes his error he bows in shame rather than blaming Arjuna for his act, which was in defence.

In my opinion —

  • Arjuna was away while the brutal hounds calling themself warriors killed him in the most cowardly way. That was hitting from the back. And Arjuna wasn’t there to protect him. Now he wasn’t going to be a moot spectator while Satyaki is killed in similarly coward faishon.
  • Bhurisrava wasn’t fighting anyone. He was going to kill Satyaki in cold-blood. Arjuna hitting him wasn’t hitting a warrior while he was engaged with other, but hitting a killer to stop him from his crime.
  • Bhurisrava was embarrassed by his own misconducts against Abhimanyu and Satyaki and bowed his head low.
  • Arjuna could have beheaded him, but he just cut his arms, protecting Satyaki but not killing him. And that is the key difference.

MOST IMPORTANT DIFFERENCE

Satyaki kills Bhurisrava in the same cowardly manner as he was about to kill him. But here we see an interesting narrative —

He was restrained by Krishna, the great-souled Partha, Bhima, the two protectors of the chariot wheels, 403 Ashvatthama, Kripa, Karna, Vrishasena and Saindhava. The soldiers loudly asked him not to kill the one who was devoted to his vows. However, Satyaki severed the head of the Indra among the Kouravas with his sword, though his arm had been severed in the battle by Partha and he was fasting to death.

—Debroy, Bibek. Mahabharata Vol. 6 (Penguin Translated Texts) (Kindle Locations 4406-4409). Penguin Books Ltd. Kindle Edition.

Everyone from Pandava’s side including Krishna and Arjuna tried to restrain Satyaki from committing such an act. This is so different from what happened with Abhimanyu. Not a single person from Kauravas side stood in defence of Dharma.

Guess there were no honourable warrior on the side of Kauravas to try restraining anyone, even their own, from comitting unrightious acts.

And that is why the two incidents are beyond comparision.
