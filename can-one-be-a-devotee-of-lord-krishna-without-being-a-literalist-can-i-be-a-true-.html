Can one be a devotee of Lord Krishna without being a literalist? Can I be a true devotee if I consider texts such as Mahabharata and Ramayana to be symbolic and allegorical rather than literal and historical?

📅 Oct 21, 2021 07:03 AM PDT  |  🌐 English

Thanks for the A2A.

Short Answer: YES.

The two things are unrelated. You don’t need to even read some text to be a devotee. That is just a state of being.

Similarly being a historical incident can also be views allegorically. It is neither unheard of nor unrealistic. Historical incidents can be seen a symbols. In fact they represent strong symbolism, why else we still debate on them. They problem is when you consider it just as symbol and nothing else. Say Rama victory over Ravana can be interpreted symbolically as internal conflict within a single human or a community. But that doesn’t mean they didn’t exist.

On the other hand, when consider Ramayana and Mahabharata as unreal and mere symbolism, may dampen your understanding of Rama and Krishna and you may end up getting trapped into your symbolism theory which would eventually but certainly lead to the question — If Ramayana and Mahabharata were mere symbolism, what were Rama and Krishna? Do they exist beyond the realm of symbolism? You may end up asking yourself — Am I being a devotee of a mere symbolism created by someone?

A greater wisdom and hence greater appreciation would come from the realization that —

  • These epics are inspired and based on real incidents.
  • Every single word in the epic may not be exactly as it occurred and few additions can’t be ruled out.
  • They do serve a great value as symbolism for several derivative ideas.
  • You may focus on symbolism without denying them as real world incidents.
