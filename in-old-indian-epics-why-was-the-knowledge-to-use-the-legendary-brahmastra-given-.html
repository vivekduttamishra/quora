In old Indian epics, why was the knowledge to use the legendary "Brahmastra" given to multiple people? Doesn't giving it to a lot of people lessen its power just like if everybody had nukes?

📅 May 20, 2022 11:56 PM PDT  |  🌐 English

Thanks for the A2A.

First things first, Divyastra like Brahmastra were nothing like modern warfare weapons like missiles on nukes. This is the result of modern unimaginative narratives or attempt to rationalize what the narrators couldn’t comprehend themselves. Those Divyastras had there own consciousness and can’t be compared to non-living machines that form our modern warfare. That is why they were not triggered by a remote and every initiation won’t have the same effect.

Second, it is a wrong notion, again propagated by modern narratives that everyone had Divyastras, and everyone had mystic bows that would manifest an arrow out of thin air. This is hyper imagination that has little value other than a being mediocre showcase of computer graphics on HD television.

Few people had the knowledge of Divyastras mostly granted only by merit — among the entire Drona’s disciple it included Arjuna and Yudhishthira. Few got the weapon as a part of their ancestral legacy like Ashwatthama and Bhagdatta and there was one known case of stealing the knowledge by deceit.

But it must be understood, that most the cases where such weapons were acquired by unworthy warriors didn’t really worked for them. Take for example, Ashwatthama, Bhagdatta or Karna. Did they work for him? No. Vaishnavastra became a garland for Krishna, Narayanastra couldn’t harm a single soldier and Bhargavastra diffused itself without harming a single Maharathi. With right usage they were capable of great feats. With wrong usage, they had not much value.

But I agree, having a lot of Divyastra lessens the chance of its misuse. A great example was a large arsenal of Divyastra with Arjuna. He didn’t use any of his higher Divyastra through out his life. And yet, they were a deterrent for the opposing camp to fire them on him.
