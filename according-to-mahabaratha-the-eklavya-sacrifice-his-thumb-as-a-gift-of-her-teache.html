There was once a bright 15 year old kid who knew more of a subject than a 10 year old kid. Unfortunately he met an accident and could not perform as great later as he was doing when he was 15.

Meanwhile the young kid continued to excell and not only became a class topper but proved is worth in his professional life as well, a lot better placed than the other guy.

So let us set aside a debate if the older guy met an accident or was punished. Let us focus on the current debate —

  • Was the older guy really better than the younger guy on day #1?
    1. Have we taken the age gap into account?
    2. Was there ever a direct competition between the two?

Unless the answer to both these question is a definite yes, it is supposition.

  • Is it guaranteed that a bright kid will always grow up to be great professionally? And no one could turn out to be better?
  • Are we sure the young kid didn't meet any accident that could have demotivated him
    • Maybe someone tried to burn him and his family alive and they suffered existential crisis?
    • Maybe someone made them suffer worst humiliation and they were exiled for years.
    • All these psychological crisis break people far worse than losing a thumb and people often have no urge to practice their capability.
  • Do we need supposition to know who is better, when we have some facts available —
    • Arjuna was ambidextrous — equally skilled with both hands. Eklavya had lost only one thumb. Did he turn out to be great with other hand at par with Arjuna?
    • Is there any record to prove Eklavya was a great warrior? Did he defeat any notable?

And most important — does talent really make a warrior?

  • I feel more important than talent, is warrior's ethics, more important than to fire a weapon is to be able to hold it back.
  • A chopped thumb can affect your talent, but not ethics? So question is how did Eklavya met his makers and why?

Eklavya may have been unfortunate, but when talent is a different aspect and ethics, even more different.
