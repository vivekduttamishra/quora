What are the 3 things I need to learn from the life of Lord Rama?

📅 Mar 28, 2020 02:08 AM PDT  |  🌐 English

Lord Ram’s Life is full of virtues and lessons for humanity and picking just 3 is like picking a glass of water from an ocean. But If I need to pick up three learning from his life it would be

  1. Submission to the divinity
    Ram is a true devotee. He derives his power from his divinity. When he follows his guru, he follows as a true desciple. When he exiled, he accepts it with a complete surrender to divinity rather than arguing for his own benefits and finding rationals and reasons (that were sufficiently available) to cancel his exile and be the King everyone wanted him to.
    His complete submission to divinity, his humility were the required quality for being selfless and so great that people can realize your true self — an incarnate.
  2. Maryada Purshottam
    Ram is maryada purshottam — he adheres to the noble conduct or protocols that should be followed, even if that comes at a great personal pains. But more important is he defines Maryada, when he finds current set of rules are not relevant. He kills Bali from behind, which was against the protocol of the era. But Rama realizes that such a protocol can’t be an excuse to let an evil live. And that sets him apart from ruler like his father (who was oath bound) or Yudhishtir who followed protocol without questioning its relevance. What made Ram Maryada Purshottam was his choice of what is Maryada.

    He could have gained the support of Bali instead of Sugriv for an easier victory over Lanka. But he realizes that he needs to leave a lesson for the society — power and personal gains are no excuses protecting or supporting evil conduct.
  3. Greatest Warrior Ever
    Krishna calls Ram as the greatest warrior when he says among warriors (wielders of weapons) I am Ram. Ram was the greatest warrior not because he could fire the most potent weapons but because he could control the urge not to fire them. Ram as a warrior knows, war and show of power should be the last resort. He is capable for drying up the ocean and cross over Lanka, but instead he prays and devices a strategty but a great lesson nonetheless. He bridges Lanka to main land. He crosses ocean and could destroy entire Lanka (even Hanuman could), but he sends Angada as emissory to see if war could be avoided and innocent Lankans could get a chance at survior. That was his war ethics. I daresay Krishna emulated that same when he reached Hastinapur court for his final appeal for avoiding the war.
