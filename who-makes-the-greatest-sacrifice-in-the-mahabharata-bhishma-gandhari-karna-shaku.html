Who makes the greatest sacrifice in the Mahabharata: Bhishma, Gandhari, Karna, Shakuni, Amba/Shikhandi, Abhimanyu, Ghatotkacha, or Eklavya?

📅 Jan 09, 2021 09:33 PM PST  |  🌐 English

This is an interesting question, however, limiting the choice to few people makes me think if we are seeking a genuine answer? And before I explain my reasons of choice, let us understand the definition of sacrifice —

The act of giving up something that you want to keep especially in order to get or do something else or to help someone

—Definition of SACRIFICE

Let us now evaluate the candidates presented here to be crowned as one who did the greatest sacrifice —

WHAT I DONT SEE AS SACRIFICE

Gandhari

What was her sacrifice? Blind folding her eyes? Who asked her to blind fold herself?Whom did it benefit? Whom did it help? Didn’t see ever realize that her blind fold is leading to her family and kingdom towards the destruction? Had she taken her blindfold off, I would have called it a great sacrifice. Her blind-fold was her solace. Her ego. But no sacrifice.

Amba/Shikhandi

Amba’s story was that of an unfortunate princess. But misfortune and sacrifice are not synonymous. Shikhandini’s story is that of revenge for Amba. She worked hard for her vengeance. Some might applaud it. But Sacrifice? Nah!

Shakuni

What was Shakuni’s sacrifice? He lived a life of luxary planning and supporting Duryodhana in his henious crimes. He with maya and deceit deprived Pandavas of their properties and diginity that led to the ultimate war. Or are we lost in those fabbles that suggest Shakuni’s story was a vengeance story for prosecution of his entire family by Hastinapur royalty. That never happened. Shakuni always had good terms with Hastinapur and specially Kauravas.

Karna

Ah! Karna. The most trending tragic hero on social media these days, I Dilip Kumar must be worried as his title is being snatched. Karna a young ambitious adopted son of Adhiratha gets the best of education by the best teachers of Aryavart. He befriends the Prince of Hastinapur and was crowned as the King of Anga at a very young age. In exchanged he vowed his loyalty to Duryodhana. He lived his entire life in luxary. Is that a sacrifice?

Some may claim that Kunti abandoned him as an infant and committed to river stream. But that can’t be considered as his sacrifice. Honestly that can’t even be considered as his misfortune — what you don’t know can’t be your fortune or misfortune. Some may blame Indra. Some may call Karna-Indra trade as a sacrifice. Fact, suggests it was a trade. But if we ignore the fact, at worst, Indra duped Karna. But it wasn’t a sacrifice.

Karna died for Duryodhana, can that be considered as sacrifice? Warriors die in battle field, that is an unwritten contract for entering the battle arena. But for Karna it was clearing-off his debt. Moreover Karna fought for his glory and where he found it being snatched, he didn’t enter the battle for first ten days.

Karna had a real chance of sacrifice — friendship and personal ambition for humanity. But…

You can’t have a piece of cake and eat it too.

Eklavya

Eklavya paid a tribute to his guru. Whether Drona was right to demand his fee, or weather Eklavaya was right to pay the fee, is a different matter. I don’t see it fitting in the definition of Sacrifice.

Again where warriors willingly sacrificed their who live, multilated and crushed, loosing a thumb can’t really be called the greatest sacrifice.

TRUE CASES OF SACRIFICE

Abhimanyu & Ghatotkacha

Abhimanyu and Ghatotkacha were great warriors, who died a hero. And in a war, whenever a warrior dies, they do it for a larger cause and that makes their death a sacrifice. That applies to every warrior who died fighting including those from Kaurava’s side like Karna, Bhagdatta and Shusharma and even the foot soldiers.

There is another side of this story. Every warrior makes a great sacrfice the moment he enter the war. Everyone knows, they may not returns. But what happens beyond that point, is part of their duty. What right or justificiation anyone can have for calling one’s sacrifice greater than the other?

While their sacrifice is great, doesn’t qualify as the greatest.

Bhishma

Now Bhishma, in my opinion is one of the great contenders for being called as sacrifice. He gave up his kingship for his father. It was beyond the call of duty. His father didn’t ask for it. It was just pure love and dedication. And only those love and dedication can make you sacrifice.

But story doesn’t end here. Unlike some, their sacrifice is a final payout and some may feel to bitter after their sacrifice to carry out, others may put a condition for the price they are paid for. But not Bhishma.

His greatest sacrifice was not relequishing the kingship, but continuing to serve his kingdom despite of all odds and never expecting anything other than safety of his family and kingdom.

He was an impenetrable protection for his family as long as he lived and made the ultimate sacrifice that his living isn’t helping the cause of his kingdom.

So among those who the question mentions, Bhishma has the tallest claim. But are we sure we haven’t missed the more worthy ones?

MOST PROMINENT OMISSIONS

Nandarai

We often forget his name from the epic. He sacrificed his new-born daughter for saving a prophesy of future welfare. In fact when people remember Krishna, they remember Yashoda but not her father. While Yashoda too lost her daughter, she had no idea. But Nandarai… Well that is something I would call as one of the greatest sacrifices.

Pandavas

There were the sons of Pandu, the true King of Hastinapur. They had claims to the throne. Yudhishtir was the crown prince when Duryodhana conspired to burn them in varnavrat. After Draupadi swaymavara they had powerful allies like Panchal and Dwarka. Yet they accepted the partition instead of sending Hastinapur to a civil war.

Their sacrifice yielded a result. They built Indraprastha, only to be looted again. They were exiled and yet on the first call they ran to protect their tormentors. That is sacrificing the well deserved revenge.

Next Pandavas grew powerful. Go not only support from Kingdoms like Panchala and Matasya, but that mighty Krishna, and grace of Shiva and Hanuman. Arjuna got the most powerful of all weapons Pashupatastra. They could have stormed their way to Hastinapur. Yet they strived for peace — sacrificing their inhertiance, their vengeance.

Krishna, the Ranchod

Krishna moved his kingdom from Mathura to dwarka. He could have just thrown his subjects to war against Jarasandha. That is what most people do. Let people die for your ego. And sacrificing you ego for others, perhaps is one of the greatest sacrifice one can make. There were warriors who fled the battle because they are afraid. And when they fled they left their loved ones behind at the mercy of their enemies. They were cowards.

But to be so powerful that you can destroy anything just by wishing and still choosing to be labelled Ranchod to remind the world virtue of letting go of your ego — that’s some sacrifice worth calling.

CONCLUSION

I would not name the ultimate sacrifice. I won’t insult others sacrifice by grading them. I just separated sacrifice from what I don’t see as sacrifice.

But understand this — greatest sacrifice is not dying or loosing a limb or kindom. In santatan culture the greatest sacrifice is — Neelkantha — someone who swallows posion for others when others seek nector of life, Amrita.

In the tradition of Neelkantha, a sacrifice is great when it comes to great personal cost for the general good — not when it is aligned to your comfort, not when it is for your personal benefit or the benefit of your friends and close allies only.
