Where is the actual original written Shrimad Bhagwad Geeta kept?

📅 Mar 28, 2020 01:11 AM PDT  |  🌐 English

Sometimes a good question is required for a great answer, and this is one such question. Remember it was a few good question that led to the sermon on Gita.

Where is original written Shrimad Bhagwat Geeta kept?

Short Answer → No where.

Remember, Krishna gave a discourse on Gita, he didn’t dicatated gita to someone. And it is true, not only for Gita but for almost entire ancient Indian wisdom.

Let us understand the most ancient Indian texts like Vedas are known as Shruti. (though I dont subscribe to the difference of Shruti and Smriti, and you will see why)

Why Shruti?

Let us understand, events of Mahabharat (and Gita) is at least 5000 years old, Ramayan is older and Vedas existed even before Ramayana era. But for Archaeology the oldest civilization is merely 3000 years old. So how do you expect to have evidence (or written text from over 5000 years old). Will the paper (or whatever medium survive (unless written on polythene!)

Consider contemporary context. When I started working with computers, we had floppy drives. Now there are no devices that can read floppy drives and it has been just 20 years. Social media like Orkut with all its information vanished. AOL, ICQ are gone. Can you ensure today's social media will live for another 20 years? We are talking about 5000 years ago.

In absence of a proper written medium (or in the wisdom that they may not be durable) we passed on the information in a hereditary fashion from one generation to another. Thus heard or Shruti.

Its only later those information where compiled to written form from the memory (Smriti). Now as I said earlier I don’t subscribe to the difference proposed by later western scholars giving lower weightage to Smriti, as they are derivative work from the same faishon.

Take example of grand epics Ramayana and Mahabharat. They are referred as Smiriti but they too originated in the same way Shurti. Remember Balmiki composed Ramayana (written) but how many copied did he write? He sent is desciples including Lav and Kush to narrate the epic and again passed to generations verbally as Shruti.

The same thing is very clearly mentioned by Krishna in the begining of chapter IV of Gita itself —

imaṁ vivasvate yogaṁ proktavān aham avyayam
vivasvān manave prāha manur ikṣhvākave ’bravīt [1]

evaṁ paramparā-prāptam imaṁ rājarṣhayo viduḥ
sa kāleneha mahatā yogo naṣhṭaḥ parantapa [2]

Which means →

I taught this eternal science of Yog to the Sun-god, Vivasvan, who passed it on to Manu; and Manu in turn instructed it to Ikshvaku.

O subduer of enemies, the saintly kings thus received this science of Yog in a continuous tradition. But with the long passage of time, it was lost to the world.

Note how Krishna refers to the ancient knowledge starting from the beginning of the time (Sun God) and how the information has been passed in continuous tradition from Sun → Manu → Ikshvaku → tradition follows. He also hints at the natural phenomenan that some knowledge also gets lost to the world in the continous process!

But essentially, passing this knowledge through a chain of human succession proved more valuable than any written record which are more easily destroyed.

So there is essentially no actual or original written part of our epic which was against the tradition and wisdom of that time.

I wish we still follow the tradition of remembering rather than depending on google and cloud to retain the knowledge, lest, one day our all accumulated knowledge may get lost.
