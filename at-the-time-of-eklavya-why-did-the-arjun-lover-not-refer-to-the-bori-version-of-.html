At the time of Eklavya, why did the Arjun lover not refer to the Bori version of the Mahabharata?

📅 Oct 30, 2021 07:32 AM PDT  |  🌐 English

The question is ambigous. Why would Arjuna fan not refer BORI for Eklavya? Let me, as a Mahabharata fan, refer about Ekalavya. Here is what BORI says —

1. Eklavya reaches Drona for learning archery. Dronacharya refused. Here Arjuna is not involved, so no reason for Aruja fan to sigh away from this incident.

2. Eklavya ignores Drona’s refusal and continues to learn archery and call him his guru. He excels in archery to such an extent that Arjuna is impressed —

Ekalavya replied, “Know me to be the son of Hiranyadhanu, the king of the nishadas. I am Drona’s student and I am trying to become skilled in Dhanur Veda.”

—Adi Parva

3. Arjuna reaches out to his guru and asks about Ekalavya —

He went to Drona and affectionately told him, “In your affection, you embraced me and told me that no pupil of yours would ever be my equal. How is it that you have another valorous pupil in this world, the son of the nishada king, who is better than everyone else?”

—Adi Parva

Note few facts —

  • Arjuna is not angry, he is curious. His tone is affectionate.
  • He doesn’t ask his guru to grant him a greater weapon so that he can defeat Ekalavaya.
  • He seeks for perfection — to be better than everyone and not some special weapons.

4. Drona meets Ekalavya and asks his right hand thumb. This transaction takes place between Drona and Ekalavya and Arjuna is not involved. So once again no need for any Arjuna fan to sigh away from this fact.

5. Now comes, perhaps the darkest phase of Arjuna’s life — Arjuna was pleased and his fever went away.

  • Yes Arjuna was envious. And he was pleased. But there are few other facts —
    • Arjuna was quite young and immature at this age.
    • Arjuna never did or asked his guru to do anything against Ekalavya.
    • We never see Arjuna being jealous of anyone throughout his life.

Arjuna was a human being, prone to human flaws. What made him great was he learnt from his mistake and learnt from it.

But the story of Ekalavya doesn’t end here. It doesn’t end with Arjuna and Drona. And it doesn’t end as the story of a dutiful pupil as generally portrayed. Ekalavya later joins Jarasandha. After the death of Jarasandha he continued to wage war against Krishna and finally met his makers —

He was challenged by Ekalavya, the king of Nishadha, who was thought to be invincible. But Krishna killed him and robbed him of his life, forcefully smashing him against a rock, like Jambha.

—Yaan Sandhi Parva, BORI

Now let me remind you, Krishna incarnated to eradicate evils and establish Dharma. Everyone who died on his hand were evil. And Ekalavya was one of them. In retrospection, it is not difficult to vindicate Drona’s action, not that it is anyway related to Arjuna.

I hope this answer should not only debunks the claims made in this question, but also throws light on the character of Ekalavya and make people think of the kind of hero they worship.
