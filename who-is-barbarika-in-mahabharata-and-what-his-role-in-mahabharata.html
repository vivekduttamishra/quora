The summary is —

  • Barbarik doesn't exist in authentic version of Mahabharata.
  • Barbarik, if exist, had a flawed notion of war — no dharma, no loyalty either, and neither had a compulsion to fight. I would fight for the weaker. What kind of skewd ideal was that?
    • Say evil is weaker, would you fight the evil?
    • Weakness and strength is dynamic. Say what party initially weaker got stronger, would you switch side.
    • Was war a game?
  • Barbarik overhyped himself and his weapon claiming he could finish off an entire army in a moment. Mind you, both sides had warriors with divine weapons like Brahmastra and Narayanastra, and to think of that an obscure weapon could finish them off is no more than a wishful thinking.
  • Barbarik is a proof of what a good marketing can achieve.
  • Krishna before removing Barbarik from the equation, proved the ineffectiveness of his weapon, that couldn't harm the leaf under his foot. Imagine what harm could it have done to army that was under his protection.
