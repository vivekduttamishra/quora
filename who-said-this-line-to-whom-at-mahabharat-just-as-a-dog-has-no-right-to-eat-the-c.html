Who said this line to whom at Mahabharat: “Just as a dog has no right to eat the cake that is offered at a sacriﬁcial ﬁre”?

📅 Oct 09, 2021 02:05 AM PDT  |  🌐 English

Great question, for this is a great opportunity to clarify some doubts that keeps propagating like a propaganda.

Let us go to the main context that lead to the incident.

1.Drona, after finishing the education of his royal patronage, decided to organize a function to demonstrate the skill the royal princess. It was an public demonstration of the of skills of princess only. It is important to note, that this wasn’t “open participation for all” just “open for all to watch”

Drona spoke to King Dhritarashtra, lord of men, in the presence of Kripa, Somadatta, Bahlika, the wise son of Ganga,1 Vyasa and Vidura, “O king! O supreme among the Kurus! The princes have now completed their learning. O king! With your permission, let them now display what they have learned.” The great king replied with gladness in his heart, “O best of the Brahmanas! O Bharadvaja! 2 You have accomplished a great task. You should yourself order me about how to arrange the display, the time and the place and about the rules to be followed. Today, I grieve for my blindness and envy men who possess eyesight. They will now be able to witness the prowess of my sons with weapons.

—Jatugrih Parva

2. Karna intrudes the event uninvited and demands to show case his skills. Now to justify his actions people forward two justifications, both of which being wrong —

A. Drona announced Arjuna to be the greatest warrior and Karna accepted this challenge. Drona did praise Arjuna as his favorite student. But he didn’t say he is the greatest archer. This shatters the first justification.

‘Then Drona entered the arena and stopped the musical instruments in a voice that was as deep as the thunder of the clouds. “Now behold Partha, who is dearer to me than my own son. He is skilled in the use of all weapons. He is Indra’s son himself and is like Indra’s younger brother.”

B. It was an open competition, which it wasn’t. The goal of the shabha was well outlined.

3.Dronacharya gracefully allows Karna to demonstrate his skills, perhaps, because he too was his student at one point.

‘With Drona’s permission, Karna, always eager to do battle and immensely strong, exhibited all that Partha had displayed before. O descendant of the Bharata lineage!

Note:

  • This also shatters the claim that Karna was denied a participation based on some caste abuse.
  • The statement is testimony of Karna’s eagerness to seek battle. There are multiple other instances to vouch for the same.

4. It wasn’t the first introduction of Karna to the royal family of Hastinapur. His father was a friend of King Dhritrashtra and he had a long term friendship with Duryodhana. It is evident from the fact that as soon as Karna enters Shabha Duryodhana and his brothers extends a warm welcome to him as one well-known to them.

Duryodhana and his brothers delightedly embraced Karna and said, “O mighty-armed hero! Welcome. Good fortune has brought you here. You know how to humble pride. I and the Kuru kingdom await your pleasure.” Karna and said, “O mighty-armed hero! Welcome. Good fortune has brought you here. You know how to humble pride. I and the Kuru kingdom await your pleasure.” Karna replied, “You have said it and that alone is enough. O descendant of the Bharata lineage! I only desire your friendship. And I desire to have a duel with Partha.”

Note:

  • contrary to popular belief and TVShows, Duryodhana doesn’t come forward to support a humiliated, distressed Karna and to give him a fair opportunity to show his skills.
  • Karna had already exhibited his skills and was as jubilant as one could be. They were old friends and no one had insulted Karna till this point.
  • Now Karna seeks to have a duel with Arjuna

5.Then Karna challenges Arjuna for a duel, something that was unprovoked and uncalled for. Kripacharya asks Karna’s lineage and not caste. And the question was evidently to dissuade him from an uncalled for battle. Here is what he says —

Kripa, well versed in all aspects of dharma and skilled in the rules of duels, said, “This son of Pandu is Kunti’s youngest son. He is a Kuru and will fight a duel with you. O mighty-armed hero! You should also tell us your mother, father and lineage and the royal dynasty of which you are the ornament. On knowing this, Partha will fight with you. Or he may not fight.

Note:

  • Kripacharya was well versed with Dharma and rule of duels.
  • He didn’t ask Karna’s caste but his lineage, his mother, father and his dynasty.
  • He asks these questions as per the rule of duel.
    • Why should someone challenge a Kuru Prince without any reason?
    • Why should a Kuru Prince fight any and sundry?

It must be noted that few questions are asked to reveal your identity to the world rather than to himself. Drona permitted Karna to showcase is talent. He didn’t feel threatened by show of his skills. But now was the question of unprovoked fight. Why shouldn’t the spectator know who seeks fight and why?

6. Duryodhana makes Karna a king to ensure he gets a chance to settle his old score. Remember there were already thick and thieves with Karna already having participated in several conspiracies against Pandavas and specifically Bhima.

Duryodhana said, “O preceptor! It is stated in the sacred texts that there are three ways to become a king—through noble birth, through valour and through leading an army. If Phalguni is unwilling to fight with someone who is not a king, I install him23 as king in the land of Anga.”

7. Now at this point Bhimasen uses those words that are mentioned in the question.

When Pandava Bhimasena saw him, he deduced that he27 was a charioteer’s son and jeeringly said, “O son of a charioteer! You don’t have the right to be killed by Partha in battle. You had better take up a whip, more befitting of your lineage. O worst of men! You have no right to enjoy the kingdom of Anga, just as a dog has no right to eat the cake that is offered at a sacrificial fire.”

Note:

  • Many, who are not well versed in English or the idea of metaphors erroneously conclude that Bhima called Karna a Dog. But those who understand metaphor would immediately recognize that Bhima called him undeserving and not Dog.
  • Dog here represents someone undeserving of sacrificial offering because they don’t have any contribution in Yagna and it would be a wrong that they would enjoy something without being worthy of it. Now I would request everyone to read read what Bhima said —
    • You have no right to enjoy the kingdom. He didn’t call him dog.
    • Then he explains it is similar to the fact that a dog has no right at sacrificial offerings.

WHY DID BHIMA HUMILIATE KARNA?

We must also realize that Bhima, who was undoubtedly harsh, wasn’t so without a good reason. Through out his childhood, he wasn’t just a witness but also a target to the various Duryodhana’s conspiracy against him and his brother and aided by Karna.

They must have felt relieved when Karna left Drona gurukul, leaving Duryodhana on his own.

Now he sees Karna returned back. Well trained. And still very much the same jealous, competing, battle hungry Karna. He intruded a private ceremony without a valid reason only to look down upon Bhima’s younger brother. And as if it wasn’t sufficient, he challenged for an unwarranted duel. Kripacharya saw and reasoned.

But then Bhimasena saw Duryodhana making him the King of Anga. Now Anga was one of the kingdom his illustrious father and the real King of Hastinapur, late Pandu had himself won. And that kingdom was being given to a young brat so that he can fulfil his lust for war.

Now tell me, how unjustifiable was Bhima’s outburst? But if you want to brandish Bhimasena as abusive, at least give him the credit that despite of multiple conspiracies against him, he still didn’t want him to die. Not yet — You don’t have the right to be killed by Partha in battle.
