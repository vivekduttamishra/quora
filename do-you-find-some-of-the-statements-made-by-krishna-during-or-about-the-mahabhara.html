Do you find some of the statements made by Krishna during or about the Mahabharata war inaccurate, wrong, or self-contradicting? If so, do you think they could have been interpolations?

📅 Jul 29, 2020 03:11 AM PDT  |  🌐 English

Thanks @Seshachalapathi Rao for A2A.

I would agree. There are few contradictions in Krishna’s statement, but what makes you think contradictions are equivalent to inaccurate or wrong?

The opposite of a fact is falsehood, but the opposite of one profound truth may very well be another profound truth.

Only a non-living object remains unchangeable. That too, not forever. Then, how can a statement remains true forever, specifically with respect to humans?

Take an example — I would say someone is sleeping, and then I would say he is eating. Now am I not contradicting myself? Am I being inaccurate or wrong? No, I contradicted myself, without being wrong. Time and context have changed.

You see, Krishna is a great teacher. And a teacher doesn’t always speak the truth. You know we have a term called proof by contradiction — where you try to prove opposite of fact, and fail doing so. Krishna has used this idea expertly.

He would often seem like praising a warrior in exaggerated term, when he would actually be provoking another in outdoing the first.

He would say it would be impossible to defeat Karna and later he would contradict that by telling that no one can save Karna today. Is this a contradiction? Sure It is. But what is the context? If Arjuna doesn’t give his best, the first statement would prove true, else…

Krishna is holestic teacher and teacher teaches everything — even contradictions. He is Ranchod, he teaches to establish dharma you need to let go your ego, forget what people thinks about you — do what is in general good. He says that if you act in my devotion, no matter what you do, is righteous. Why because a work dedicated to a bigger cause is devoid of selfish interest.

About interpolations, yes there are several points that are interpolations. But we can’t use this word as cover to ditch uncomfortable questions. Neither can we use our own moral yardstick to judge Krishna — the ultimate teacher.
