Did Karna support it?

That would be a gross UNDERSTATEMENT.

Karna orchestrated it. Dussassana was just carrying out the bid. It wasn’t Duryodhana or Shakuni either. It was an out-and-out Karna’s show — written and directed by him. It was his dirty mind where this heinous idea actually germinated from the beginning to the end and beyond —

If you think that bringing her into the sabha when she is clad in only a single garment is against dharma, listen to the words I have to say in response. O descendant of the Kuru lineage!213 It has been ordained by the gods that a woman should only have one husband. However, she submits to many and it is therefore certain that she is a courtesan. It is my view that there is nothing surprising in her being brought into the sabha in a single garment, or even if she is naked. In accordance with dharma, Soubala has won all the riches the Pandavas possessed, including her and themselves. O Duhshasana! This Vikarna is only a child, though he speaks words of wisdom. Strip away the garments from the Pandavas and Droupadi.”

—Debroy, Bibek. Mahabharata Vol. 2 (Kindle Locations 3374-3379).

Hering the command from great Karna, Dushasana carried out the failed effort.

O descendant of the Bharata lineage! On hearing these words, the Pandavas took off their upper garments and sat down in the sabha. O king! Then Duhshasana forcibly tugged at Droupadi’s garments. In front of everyone in the sabha, he forcibly pulled.

—Debroy, Bibek. Mahabharata Vol. 2 (Kindle Locations 3379-3381).

And the story doesn’t end here. Dussassana failed and stopped here. The beast in Karna was unstoppable —

‘Karna said, “There are three who can own no property—a slave, a student and a woman. O fortunate one! You are the wife of a slave and have nothing of your own. You have no lord and are like the property of slaves. Enter226 and serve us. That is the task for you in this household. O Princess! All the sons of Dhritarashtra are now your masters and not the sons of Pritha. O beautiful one! Choose another one for your husband, one who will not make you a slave through gambling. Remember the eternal rule among slaves. Sexual acts with one’s masters are never censured. Nakula, Bhimasena, Yudhishthira, Sahadeva and Arjuna have been won over. O Yajnaseni! Enter as a slave. The ones who have been won over can no longer be your husbands. Valour and virility are of no use to Partha227 now.

—Debroy, Bibek. Mahabharata Vol. 2 (Penguin Translated Texts) (Kindle Locations 3468-3474). Penguin Books Ltd. Kindle Edition.


WAIT ISN’T VASTRAHARAN AN INTERPOLATION

Karna’s role in vastraharana is so explicit and so brutal, that no one can justify it. Not even most adherent fan of Karna can defend his act. And the only option is to redact the incident. And that people try now — Vastraharana was an interpolation!

So how does it become an interpolation?

Few scholars raised doubts — with their usual maybe and perhaps. Their entire logic is based on a single premise — While everyone mentions Draupadi was dragged to assembly, there are only few mention of stripping.

Naturally. Why would you mention a tormenting experience that failed. They can’t say stripping happened. Because it didn’t. It failed. But that doesn’t mean it was not attempted.

But most important…

Even so called scholars don’t call the entire dyut incident an interpolation. Even they don’t refer to Karna’s role as an interpolation. Not one of them.


But Karna repented for his role. Right?

Well, yes. Karna said, he repented. And this repentance of Karna actually validates his role in Draupadi’s humiliation beyond reproach.

But is repentance just a word or an expression? Or is it should reflect even in action? And that is an important question.

Had Karna repented his actions in Draupadi’s swyamvara, he wouldn’t have come up with this plan years later —

O tiger among kings! The birth of a son or the obtaining of a kingdom does not bring as much bliss as seeing one’s enemies in misery. What happiness will there not be, on being successful oneself, and on seeing Dhananjaya attired in bark and skins? Let your wives, attired in excellent garments, see the miserable Krishna, attired in bark and skins, and thereby increase her grief.

—Debroy, Bibek. Mahabharata Vol. 3 (Kindle Locations 6283-6286).

We must realize, Draupadi’s humilation by Karna at Dyutshabha wasn’t an isolated incident. It wasn’t the first one. And it wasn’t the last one. It was one in a series of incidents that exposes his true character.
