Why did Arjuna steal the five golden arrows of Bhishma from Duryodhana in the Kurukshetra War?

📅 Oct 30, 2021 11:10 PM PDT  |  🌐 English

Arjuna stole those give golden arrows of Bhishma from Duryodhana because maybe he had run out of the toothpick and needed them badly. Or maybe he wanted to protect Duryodhana from dying in case those arrow boomranged. Or maybe Arjuna wanted to loan them to Karna so that he can melt it to make his golden tooth that he would later donate to Krishna after Arjuna cuts off his head.

DID YOU FIND THESE ANSWER ILLOGICAL?

Well they are more logical or at least as logical as the episode of five golden arrows. A seer figment of imagination. To imagine that a deceitful thug who spent his life in conspiracy, plunder, and fear would first give Arjuna a boon and then go out of the way to fulfil it, is not in the realm of creative imagination, but creative bankruptcy.

THE FICTIOUS STORY OF THE FIVE ARROWS.

After Duryodhana through his deceit, manages to humiliate banish Pandavas and Draupadi to the to exile, on the advice of Karna decides to pay them a visit in exile, to mock them and aggrevate their miseries. Indra sends Gandharvas to capture them. Duryodhana and his army was captures when his friend abandoning him flees the battle. Again.

At the Yudhishthir's command, Arjuna along with other three Pandavas approach Gandharvas to secure the release of Kauravas and when the peaceful parley fails, they secure their release after a terrible battle which ended in Chitrasena the Chief of Gandharvas surrendering against Arjuna.

Now as per the fictious narrative, Duryodhana feels an obligation towards Arjuna and asks him generously to ask him anything in return, which Arjuna reserves for a future time with Duryodhana’s solemn promise to honor his words.

Flash forward to Kurukshetra. Duryodhana finds Bhishma reluctant to kill Pandavas. He confronts him with harsh words and Bhishma in a feet of anger picks five golden arrow and arm them with mantra promising he could use these arrow to kill all the five Pandavas on the next day of battle.

But the deceitful Krishna gets a wind of the plot and crooked that he was, asks Arjuna to ask Duryodhana to fulfil his pledge by giving away the five arrow. Which of course the honorable Duryodhana complied despite of knowing that it would drag him away from his life long dream.

WHY IS THIS STORY ABSURD.

To use the word ‘Absurd’ would be using a mild word for this creative bankruptcy. It fails on more accounts that the number of word it actually contains —

  1. Imagine a deceitful man who from his early childhood was involved in greed and plot of murders. He is a back stabber, arsonist, who knows his incapability and uses deceit to rob Pandavas of their prosperity. And he immediately turns saintly? Well to imagine a heinous demon turns saint is not unheard of.
  2. In line to his character, he sets out to mock Pandavas where they were outpowered by Gandharvas only to be rescued by them for whom Duryodhana harbored only envy and ill-will. Now does he develop a feeling of gratitude? Any normal human would feel and such the story remain in logical realm. So he offers a boon to show his gratitude. Now the question arises —
    1. Why didn’t Duryodhana offered a similar boons to other Pandavas, who too played active part in his rescue?
    2. Had Duryodhana really turned to a saint, why didn’t he realize his fault and returned rightful share of Pandavas he so unrightfully usurped?
  3. Now let us turn to Arjuna. He plays a Kekai and reserves his right to ask for his boon at the appropriate time. The Generous Duryodhna grants the boon, which begs the questions —
    1. If Arjuna indeed wanted to ask for a boon, why didn’t he ask it right away? He could have demanded his rightful share and Duryodhana was bound to give. Right?
    2. What could have been a more appropriate time in Arjuna’s opinion that he didn’t ask the boon, didn’t decline such an offer, simply reserves for the future?
  4. Fast forward thirteen years —
    1. imagine the same generous Duryodhana refused to give a land share equivalent to the size a niddle head. Why didn’t Arjuna asked for five village in exchange to Duryodhana’s former pledge? Or why didn’t he simply demand the entire kingdom? There was no limit placed on his pledge by Duryodhana. Was it?
  5. In to the battle field, Duryodhana agitates Bhishma and he manifests five golden arrows to kill five Pandavas. Now does it fit the character of Bhishma?
    1. He who never ever wavered from his resolution all his life, remained a bachelor and didn’t lust for the throne. How would he break his own resolution which he made clear to Duryodhana before the battle — He will not kill Pandavas?
    2. How could Duryodhana have mistrusted Bhishma to have taken the arrow in his custody, when he chose him over even his best friend to command his army? Is it conceivable?
  6. Krishna and Arjuna plans of robbing Duryodhana of the five arrows. Now here is where the story becomes most absurd —
    1. Why did Arjuna just asked for five golden arrows and not a direct surrender? That could have saved everyone’s hassle? Now it can’t be Arjuna’s ego, because his approach to Duryodhana was no less than a complete surrender. Right?
    2. How could Arjuna be sure that Bhishma won’t manifest another five arrows and Arjuna would have been fooled by using his only pledge on just five arrows?
    3. How could Duryodhana so blinded by hatred and lust for power that he didn’t hesitate in hackling the son of his protector to death, would have given away only means of his victory and life?
  7. Finally let us turn our attention to fabled five arrows itself. How much powerful those arrows might have been to actually make Krishna worry? Were they likely to be more powerful than Bhramasira and Pashupata? Were they more powerful than Vaishnavastra, that Krishna couldn’t have intervened?

IS THERE NO TRUTH IN THE STORY

Sure there is. Even the dead clock is correct twice a day, even if my seer coincidence. Here are those occasions —

  1. Duryodhana indeed had gone mock Pandavas where they were busted by Gandharvas and rescued by Arjuna.
  2. Duyrodhana did feel humiliated (not guilty) even contemplating suicide. It was one moment where Duryodhana reflected upon his evil design. One point, however, Duryodhana wasn’t really please by Pandavas but rather found their intrusion insulting. He would have preferred death over disgrace. It is unlikely a person with such a mean mind would grant anyone any wish.
  3. Karna convinces Duryodhana that as a warrior subject of the Kingdom, it was a duty of Pandavas to help their distressed King and Pandava’s did not great favor. Duryodhana was convinced — A proof of that fact is his battle against Arjuna at Matasya, his point blank refusal to give the rightful dues of Pandavas and his waging the war. Some people never change.

WHY IS THIS STORY CREATED

This story is a wishful thinking that a evil like Duryodhana may have an honorable side. There existed none. This story is also a distressed attempt to malign the character of Arjuna and above all Krishna, whom they want to portray as a conniving deceitful person. There is a popular saying, if you can’t rise to someone level, try to pull it down. This story is a lame attempt at the same.
