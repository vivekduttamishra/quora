Was Mahatma Gandhi really supporting non-violence? If yes, then why did he allow Indian soldiers to take part in the World War? Why did he support the Khilafat movement?

📅 Apr 06, 2021 11:20 AM PDT  |  🌐 English

The question clearly shows the lack of understanding of the concept of non-violence. People are somehow confused between non-violence and cowardice. I can assure you they are two very very different things. To understand the concept better let me ask you another set of questions —

KRISHNA, MAHABHARATA AND GITA

Why did Krishna moved his kingdom from Mathura to Dwarka? Why didn’t he asked his people to take up arms and fight an armed battle against Jarasandha? Because that would have been a foolhardy. Think again, why did he become Pandava’s emissary and pushed forward for a peaceful resolution even if it means unjustified compromise — just five villages instead of the entire kingdom? Was Krishna a coward? No. But only fools prefer senseless violence over peace.

Now you may ask why the same peace-loving Krishna motivated Arjuna to fight when he talked against war? Why did he preach Gita and asked Arjuna to fight? Because even after you tried to avoid the war, when the war is knocking at your door, your refusal to fight is no more a a question of violence and non-violance, but bravery, cowardise and above all your duty.

BACK TO MAHATMA GANDHI

Now can you draw a parallel of this story with Mahatma Gandhi’s act? Let me quote you Gandhiji’s view which is often shoved under the rug by his critics. This si what Gandhiji said in 1920 —

I do believe that, where there is only a choice between cowardice and violence, I would advise violence... I would rather have India resort to arms in order to defend her honour than that she should, in a cowardly manner, become or remain a helpless witness to her own dishonor. But I believe that nonviolence is infinitely superior to violence, forgiveness is more manly than punishment.

—Mahatma Gandhi

As you can clearly see, In Mahatma’s vision non-violence is superior to violence and violence is superior to cowardice, and the nation's honor is paramount. But the preacher of violence had the foresight to realize people would misconstrue his statement, so he once again explicitly said in 1938 —

"For I cannot in any case tolerate cowardice. Let no one say when I am gone that I taught the people to be cowards... I would far rather that you died bravely dealing a blow and receiving a blow than died in abject terror...fleeing from battle is cowardice and unworthy of a warrior... cowardice is worse than violence because cowards can never be non-violent."

—Mahatma Gandhi

I believe that should provide you the answer directly from Mahatma’s mouth. It must be understood that Mahatma Gandhi was not the army general to command someone to fight or not, but as a person who keeps nation paramount, he would certainly advised non-violence over cowardice.

Many believe that Gandhi’s preaching contradicts Krishna’s teaching. But if you see the evidence you find them exactly the same.


KHILAFAT MOVEMENT

That is one of the common arsenal Gandhian critics think they have against Mahatma Gandhi. Most likely they haven’t heard about the famous Divide and Rule policy. Most likely they forgot the partition of Bengal. India would not be independent without Indian’s effort — certainly not if we would be busy fighting amongst ourselves acting as a puppet in British hand. And that was the reason our best movements were simply ineffective.

Khilafat movement was a masterstroke. It had nothing to do with India. And common-sense logic says we had nothing to gain in a movement that is not associated with India. And yet it was a masterstroke. It help freedom movement capitalize on a joint Hindu-Muslim collaboration that was never seen before. It also put additional pressure of British government who realized new threat to their colonial rule — an international alliance that could make governance more difficult.

Unfortunately few see even pro-india movement with racial divide and this criticism is a glaring example.
