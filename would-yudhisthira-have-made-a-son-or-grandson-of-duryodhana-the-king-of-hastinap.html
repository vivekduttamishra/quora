Though such a foolish idealism can be expected from a thirteen or fourteen year younger Yudhishthir, it can be said with utmost certainty that Yudhisthir who won Kurukshetra war would have done no such thing.

Thirteen years ago an idealistic young King failed to distinguish between protocol (Parampara) and Dharma and gambled away his kingdom. He also missed the fact that Dharma isn't dependent on useless facts that few call truth. He saw his wife humiliated in open court.

But thirteen years of exile had made him more matured. He learnt to hide facts if it harms no one. The washed away gambler lived a whole year disguised as an accomplished gambler under a psudo name and even agreed to convince Dronacharya that Ashwathama was dead.

Yudhisthir would never have gambled his kingdom ever again. And a clear proof is the fact that he ruled Hastinapur despite of the fact that one son of Dhritarashtra survived the battle.

But an important question is,

why shouldn't Yudhisthir have given Hastinapur throne to any successor of Duryodhana?

Let us ask, why should Yudhisthir have done that?

  1. Hastinapur wasn't Duryodhana's by right. The true king of Hastinapur was Pandu. It was Dhritarashtra’s cunning, Duryodhana's ambition, and Pandava's compromising attitude that they got a bad deal.
  2. Even if we assume Pandavas agreed to the partition, that was no longer relevant after Kurukshetra war. Duryodhana forfeit all his claims, moment he declined to accept old treaty, and resorted to war to decide who has right over Hastinapur.
  3. Had Yudhisthir done that, it would be an insult to all martyrs of war, who died to make him King and establish the rule of righteousness.
  4. It would be legitimizing Duryodhana's claim to the throne, which wasn't true.
