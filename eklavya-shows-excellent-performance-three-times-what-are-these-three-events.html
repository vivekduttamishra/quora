Eklavya shows excellent performance three times. what are these three events?

📅 Nov 27, 2021 11:00 PM PST  |  🌐 English

Define excellent performance.

There are references of a total of three phases of Eklavya’s life in the authentic epic. And here are they —

1. The young Eklavya seeks learning from Dronacharya

Dronacharya refuses to accept Ekalavya as his student in most explicit terms. That didn’t deter Ekalvya from either archery or calling Dronacharya as his guru against his explicit wishes. His proximity to gurukul also indicates (without a direct reference) that he continued to learn from Dronacharya from a distance.

But his performance had been excellent and reached the zenith as his feat of attacking a dog with seven arrows continued to echo even after five thousand years. This proves he had turned out to be an excellent archer. But does that also prove he was an excellent warrior with warrior ethics? Future will tell.

Now comes what is marked as the gory incident of Drona’s career. He asks for Eklavya’s thumb for impersonating himself as his disciple. Some may feel Drona had no right to demand a preceptor's fee if he didn’t teach anything. Some may even say he had no right as to whom he would teach or impart his knowledge. Some may say Drona had no right to his own knowledge or punish anyone who misuses his name. But this is not a question related to Drona so let us leave Drona here with his blemished name and return to Eklavya and follow his career.

2. Rajsuy Yagna battle with Bhimasena

When Yudhisthira performed the Rajsuy yagna the four Pandava’s went in four direction to bring rulers under his rule. Bhimasena marched towards east subjugating rulers, Bhimasena defeated Nishada’s (ruled by Ekalavya), Jarasandha’s son and Karna —

The strong Kounteya used his strength to defeat the land of the Vatsas, the lord of the Bhargas, the lord of the Nishadas and many other owners of the land, led by Manimana.

—Rajsuy Yagna Parva

This incident certainly doesn’t vouch for great performance by Ekalayva. But can we be sure that Nishada defeated by Bhimasena was Ekalavya. Actually we may not. He may have been defeated by Sahadeva in his expedition to south, where he too defeated a Nishada kingdom —

Vaishampayana said, ‘In similar fashion, after worshipping Dharmaraja, Sahadeva left for the south with a large army. The lord first conquered the entire land of the Shurasenas. 45 The descendant of the Kuru lineage, supreme among powerful ones, then subjugated the king of Matsya. 46 In a great battle, he defeated Dantavakra, emperor among kings. Having forced him to pay tribute, he restored the throne to him. He then vanquished Sukumara and King Sumitra and conquered the other Matsyas and the Patachcharas. The illustrious one swiftly conquered the country of the nishadas and Goshringa, supreme among mountains. Then he defeated King Shrenimana.

— Rajsuy Yagna

But with Ekalvya’s proximity with Jarasandha points more toward Bhimasena. But is it possible that neither were ruled by Ekalayva? No. Ekalvya was clearly subjugated and it’s proof is mentioned in the Rajsuy Parva, where he was in attendance holding Yudhishthira’s shoes —

The king from the south had the armour ready, Magadha88 the garland and the headdress. The great archer Vasudana89 held the king of elephants, sixty years old. Matsya90 fixed the sides,91 Ekalavya held the footwear. Avanti had the many kinds of water required for the final bath. Chekitana92 gave the quiver, the king of Kashi the bow and Shalya the sword with a golden hilt and with straps inlaid with gold.

— Rajsuy Yagna

3. His death at the hands of Krishna

Eklavya was a confederate of Jarasandha and he continued to attack Dwarka long after the death of Jarasandha. Finally he met his maker at the hand of Krishna.

He (Krishna) was challenged by Ekalavya, the king of Nishadha, who was thought to be invincible. But Krishna killed him and robbed him of his life, forcefully smashing him against a rock, like Jambha.

—Yana Sandhi Parva

Jarasandha was one of the most ruthless, evil and powerful King of his era who was killed by Bhimasena. Krishna killed Ekalavya and Krishna himself proclaimed he manifested to destroy evils and protect the good. So where does this place Ekalayva? If still in doubt, check out Krishna statement —

The immensely strong Ekalavya from Nishadha—all of these have been killed through my yoga, for your sake. 3 Later, other Indras among rakshasas have been killed—Hidimba, Kirmira, Baka, the foremost among them, Alayudha, the destroyer of enemy soldiers, and the spirited Ghatotkacha, the performer of fierce deeds.’”’

—Drona Vadha Parva

Krishna tells Arjuna, I killed him for you sake. And then he mentions other demons that too were killed in a similar fashion — Hidimba, Kimira, Baka etc. Why did Krishna killed Ekalavya for Arjuna’s sake? Naturally Ekalavya, if alive, would have fought on behalf of Kauravas. Krishna always maintained Arjuna to be inseparable part of his existence as such their goals were clearly aligned.
