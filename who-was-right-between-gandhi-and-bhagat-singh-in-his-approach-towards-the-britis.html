Who was right between Gandhi and Bhagat Singh in his approach towards the British rule in India?

📅 Feb 06, 2022 11:57 PM PST  |  🌐 English

I find it is a dangerous idea to pit our great leaders and martyrs against each others. No matter who wins, we always lose the debate.

Both Bhagat Singh and Mahatma Gandhi were sincere and patriots of the highest order and dedicated their lives for us. Both died for us. They followed their ideals.

But to answer the question, which approach was right and which was wrong, we must ask another vital question. Were their attempts really different or in contrast?

Let us start with Bhagat Singh. He was born in a family of revolutionaries. Legend says, he buried a toy gun saying he is seeding the tree of guns and once the tree flourishes, we woul bold have sufficient ammunition's to drive Britishers away. He was involved in attempt to avenge Lala Lajpat Rai’s death. And yet, the same Bhagat Singh dropped a bomb at the assembly hall with the intent not to kill anyone. Why did Bhagat Singh do that? Why did he resolve to non-violence? Why didn’t he escape? He had the opportunity.

He wanted his ideals to be heard. Ideals. Not the gunshot. The problem was, as a revolutionary Britishers were chasing them like hounds. They were grossly misunderstood even by common people. Many robbers and looters, posing as members of HSRA were maligning their reputation.

When the deciding factor in a war is not ideals but guns, the victory is decided solely by who has got better, bigger and more guns. And Britishers had more guns. Revolutionaries like Bhagat Singh and Azad were out numbered, with insufficient fund to buy guns or opportunity to procure powerful weapons. Bhagwati Charan Vohra, the great revolutionary died while trying to experiment with a crude bomb. Great Chandrashekhar Azad, was shot down brutally by a large troop. What does this tale tell you? Let us take more examples — INA, a formal army couldn’t even reach Indian borders. In modern day LTTE was one of the largest private army that was wiped out. The whole might of a nation couldn’t protect Saddam Hussain from prosecution.

If deciding factor is a gun, those with larger and more powerful gun wins. The ideology don’t count.

Once Bhagat Singh realized the futility of the situation, he came out of incognito action. He made the court room his platform to spread his ideas, much to the discomfort of British government. He got a platform, no other revolutionary ever got. But that was not with the gun, but by surrendering it. It was this brilliant strategy that people realized the great heroic attempts of revolutionaries like Azad and Bismil. Bhagat Sing further used his remaining time in exposing the autocracies in British Prison and went on to fast on to the death. The mighty administration had to bow down to stop a man from starving — the world was watching.

So now tell me, how Bhagat Singh’s ideas different from Mahatma Gandhi’s? How his throwing of a non-harming bomb different from Mahatma burning boycotting foreign goods? How was his court trials different from Mahatmas? How is his fasting different from Mahatma’s fasting?

Bhagat Singh wasn’t given a capital punishment for throwing a harmless bomb in assembly hall. He couldn’t have been given death sentence and he would still be in a position to guide the youth with his newly acquired vision. He was sentenced to death for his earlier action involving killing of John Saunders.

So, if you look closely the success story of both Gandhiji and Bhagat Singh followed the same path — focus of ideals and not on violence. The violent protest can be suppressed with greater violence, but moral demand following ideals is difficult to suppress.

As I see it, Bhagat Singh was a young man with fiery spirit fueled by revolutionary thoughts and like every young man in early twenties lacked maturity. His actions were more the product of his great nationalism and not backed by same rationale thought. Their original plan was to kill police superintendent James Scott and ended up killing Saunders. We may call it intelligence failure or improper planning. But then we see a different Bhagat Singh emerge - no less in his patriotic ideas, but a braver one. This Bhagat Singh had become more matured. He was ready to challenge the British raaj by a harmless non-violent bomb. The first Bhagat Singh along with his friends were forced to live a life in hiding and incognito, but the Bhagat Singh who got the attention of the masses was the one who stood tall, dwarfing the entire Britishraaj.

And in the later Bhagat Singh, The ideals of Gandhiji is too apparent to miss.
