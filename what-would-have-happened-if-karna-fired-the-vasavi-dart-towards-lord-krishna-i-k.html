What would have happened if Karna fired the Vasavi dart towards Lord Krishna? I know Lord Krishna decided everything, but just in a hypothetical situation, what would happen?

📅 Sep 27, 2020 07:34 AM PDT  |  🌐 English

Remember what happened when —

  • Aswhatthama fired Naraynastra?
  • Bhagdatta fired Vaishnavastra?

They successfully fired those weapons. Didn’t they? Narayanastra returned without harming Pandavas. Vaishnavastra garlanded Krishna.

So having established the credential of Krishna and his tryst with celestial weapons, let us dive into the hypothetical situation and find a very plausible answer to —

If Karna fired Vashvi dart targetting Krishna

I could offer three very very likely solutions —

  1. Vashvi dart would have hurt Karna badly — Remember when Indra offered Karna Vashavi shakti he had explained its usage conditions. And here is the condition —

But if you unleash this invincible weapon in a fit of fury, when you possess other weapons, there is no doubt that it will descend on you.”

—Debroy, Bibek. Mahabharata Vol. 3 (Kindle Locations 8561-8562).

Now Krishna was not a combatant and wasn’t firing any weapons. So any attack on an non-combatant naturally falls under the category of fit of fury. So Karna was most likely to be at the receiving end.

2. Arjuna had Pashupatastra — I know, this option may raise eyebrows of many critics who are ready to condemn Pashupatastra the weapon of Mahadeva as just any other weapon. But no other weapon in Mahabharata or any other text is mentioned that has known capacity to annihilate entire universe, with everything. So Arjuna may have encountered Vashvi weapon with Pashupatastra. It is probably to avoid such an extreme situation, Krishna kept distance from Vashvi dart — avoid such an extreme collision.

3. Vashvi dart would have done absolutely nothing to Krishna — You may call it my blind faith, but it is endorsed by an explicit Indra’s statement —

Indra said, “You kill one roaring and powerful enemy in battle. But the one you seek to kill is protected by a great-souled one who is known as the unvanquished boar,38 Hari and the inconceivable Narayana by those who are learned in the Vedas.”

—Debroy, Bibek. Mahabharata Vol. 3 (Penguin Translated Texts) (Kindle Locations 8555-8557). Penguin Books Ltd. Kindle Edition.

As you can see, Indra had already fore-warned Karna that the Vasvi dart might not be able to kill Arjuna, as he is protected by unvanquished and inconceivable Narayana.

And here we are talking about what Vashvi dart might do to him — the unvanquished and inconceivable Narayana! Even Indra didn’t see his dart harming him.

Side Note on Divyastra’s Standard Operating Procedure.

Many underestimate Vashvi Shakti of Indra and indicate that Indra cheated Karna by giving him Vashvi Shakti that had a lots of terms and conditions to its use. It is important to understand that these terms and conditions were part of standard operating procedure of the use of such a weapon that had be explained by Dronacharya and Mahadeva Shiva while bestowing higher weapons. Here are the references —

  • Drona’s warning to Arjuna while handing him Brahmashira.

Bharadvaja’s son told the great-souled maharatha, “O mighty-armed one! Receive this invincible and supreme weapon, named brahmashira, with the knowledge of releasing it and withdrawing it. You must never use it against human beings. If it is used against an enemy whose energy is inferior, it will burn up the entire universe.

—Debroy, Bibek. Mahabharata Vol. 1 (Penguin Translated Texts) (Kindle Locations 5106-5108). Penguin Books Ltd. Kindle Edition.

  • Mahadeva Shiva’s warning to Arjuna while handing him Pashupatastra

Partha! But it must not be suddenly released at any man. If it is released at someone who lacks in energy, it will destroy the entire universe. There is no one in the three worlds, mobile or immobile, who cannot be killed by it.

—Debroy, Bibek. Mahabharata Vol. 2 (Penguin Translated Texts) (Kindle Locations 5484-5486). Penguin Books Ltd. Kindle Edition.

If you compare these statements to what Indra said, you will find that they all gave the same warning with one key difference —

  • Shiva and Dronacharya warned Arjuna that the weapon might destroy the world. They knew this is sufficient a warning to keep Arjuna from mis-using it.
  • Indra changed his warning and included personal harm to Karna. This is perhaps because he realized that personal harm would be a greater deterrent to Karna than the consideration of universal well being.

And this is the same warning which Parshurama gave to Karna. Only thing, it is often referred as a curse. Interestingly, authentic text doesn’t use the term curse, nor is the expression of Parshurma —

However, he smiled and said, ‘You acted in this false way because of your greed for weapons. O stupid one! In a different place, when the time for your death has come, you will be engaged in a fight with someone who is your equal and the brahmastra will not manifest itself before you. The qualities of a brahmana will never remain with someone who is not a brahmana. Leave this place, since this is not meant for an untruthful one like you.

—Debroy, Bibek. Mahabharata: Volume 8 (Kindle Locations 2403-2406). Penguin Books Ltd. Kindle Edition.

What Parshurama said is more like a prediction understanding the nature of the divine weapon rather than a curse. He is not angry. He is smiling, knowing that weapon that Karna acquired by deceiving him, won’t work for him. Yet it is often referred as curse. Even KMG mentions Parshurma’s smiling here and doesn’t include the word cursed.
