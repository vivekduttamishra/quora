Let me divide your question in two distinct parts —

Why there is so much distortion or differences in versions of Mahabharata.

First, let me emphasize that there is a difference between variation and distortion and the epic suffers from both of them.

Mahabharata, the grand epic, is based on an incident that occurred over five thousand years ago and didn’t exist in written form since it’s inception. What is important to note is that no written media (ink or surface) can survive five thousand years. They would be destroyed by nature and invaders.

Our great culture ensured their survival using magnificent Shruti-Smriti tradition that ensured it’s survival of the epic narrative, transferring through a unqiue neural network — generations of human chain. This was one medium that was immune to the damages caused nature of invaders — you can’t erase it from all human brains.

But this apporach allowed additions by human narrators — sometimes due to their own creative imaginations and sometimes simply because they don’t remember the exact details.

Some of these alternations just make the narrative richer and interesting without killing the essence of what happened and why it must be remembered — A great example would be inclusion of Lakshman Rekha in Ramayana. While Lakshman Rekha inserts a subtle message for family safety, it doesn’t kill the essence of epic. We would call such narratives as mere variations or positive addons.

But then there are other narratives, that we should be worried about — they distort the essence of the epic. They present a contrary tale and often contradict the epics main essence. We should be careful against such distortions.

You may be wondering if this problem persists because the epic was not originally written and may not have occured had it been written on the day one. Well, this would be a wrong assumption. The variation or distortions is not the result of non-written nature of the work, rather it is the outcome of ever creative (and destructive) human imagination.

Even if it were written, say in Sanskrit, there would have been an English translation. And that translation may alter the text — sometimes due to limitations of the translator or the language in which it is translated or because of his own assumptions which may prove stronger than the original texts — we have several instances of flawed translation of Sanskrit verses into it’s English translated version.

Second a written work doesn’t guarantee, no one would ever temper with it. Consider this — All written works on Mahabharata clearly mentions that Karna was taught by Drona and Kripacharya. Did this ever stop mordern narratives from telling that Karna was rejected by Drona?

Having set the base let us come to the second half of the question —

Was there no need to bend and see the reflection of target in water and was the target not moving as per BORI?

Neither BORI nor ancient Neelkantha mentions any specifics of the nature of target that was to brought down other than the fact that it had to be brought down using five arrows.

Here is what BORI says —

Hear that this is the bow, that is the target and these are the arrows. With these five arrows, shoot the target through the hole in the centre of the machine. I truly say that the handsome and strong one, born in a noble family, who accomplishes this great feat today, will obtain as his wife my sister Krishna.”

In accordance Arjuna hits the target and brings it down

the scorcher of enemies bowed his head to the bow and joyously grasped it. In the twinkling of an eye, he strung the bow and grasped the five arrows. Through the hole in the machine, he suddenly pierced the target and it fell down on the ground.

Same is narrated in KMG (translated from Neelkantha) this way

'Hear ye assembled kings, this is the bow, that is the mark, and these are the arrows. Shoot the mark through the orifice of the machine with these five sharpened arrows. Truly do I say that, possessed of lineage, beauty of persons, and strength whoever achieveth this great feat shall obtain today this my sister, Krishna for his wife.' Having thus spoken unto the assembled monarchs Drupada's son then addressed his sister, reciting unto her the names and lineages and achievements of those assembled lords of the earth.'"

And here is how Arjuna brings down the target.

And that bow which Rukma, Sunitha, Vakra, Radha's son, Duryodhana, Salya, and many other kings accomplished in the science and practice of arms, could not even with great exertion, string, Arjuna, the son of Indra, that foremost of all persons endued with energy and like unto the younger brother of Indra (Vishnu) in might, strung in the twinkling of an eye. And taking up the five arrows he shot the mark and caused it to fall down on the ground through the hole in the machine above which it had been placed.

Now none of these narratives mention —

  • A divice that looked like a fish
  • Any mention of reflection in water or any ripple in those water.

Now I would say, if some creative brain creates the exact design of divice and presents a greater visualization, this is an innocent effort to make the story more visual and creative and it doesn’t contradict the epic narrative which doesn’t include any such details.

What would be considered in the realm of distortion is when you suggest that Krishna helped Arjuna by his divine power so that he can shot down the target or that He advised Draupadi to reject Karna. These are destortion because it is clearly seen that Krishna was sitting with other guests and not in a special pavallian. There is no evidence that he ever met Draupadi. And a contradiction exists in the fact that Karna failed in swyamvara which is clearly mentioned in both ancient and critical edition.

Hope this answers the questions.
