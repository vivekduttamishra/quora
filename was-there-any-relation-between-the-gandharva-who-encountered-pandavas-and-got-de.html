Was there any relation between the Gandharva who encountered Pandavas and got defeated by ARJUNA in Adiparva and the one who tried to abduct Duryodhana during Ghoshayatra? Is hethe same Gandharva as per some versions of Mahabharaha?

📅 Nov 30, 2021 07:51 AM PST  |  🌐 English

Short Answer: NO.

Now coming to the question. The two Gandharavas are decidedly different and I couldn’t find any relation between the two, but beyond doubt they are distinct.

Let me give a brief profile of the two Gandharvas starting with the later, the more popular Chitrasena.


CHITRASENA

Chitrasena is the more popular of two Gandharvas. He was the son of the King of Gandharva named Vishvavasu’s and a close confidant of Indra. He had two relationship with Arjuna. He was his guru and a dear friend. These relations are first mentioned in Indralokagamana Parva —

On receiving the weapon, Pandava Kounteya thought of his brothers. But on Purandara’s instructions, he lived there happily for five years. When Partha became skilled in the use of all weapons and the right time had come, Shakra told him, “O Kounteya! Now learn singing and dancing from Chitrasena. Learn the music that is only known to the gods and is unknown in the world of men. O Kounteya! If you learn this, it will bring your own welfare.” Purandara then gave him to Chitrasena as a friend. Partha lived happily with him, free from all disease.

—Indraloka Gamana Parva

We see him referred next in Yaksha Yudha Parva. However that must be seen as a continuation of the earlier incident. Both this provide reference of closer relationship between Arjuna and Chitrasena

O king! Vishvavasu’s178 son Chitrasena became my friend and he taught me everything that the gandharvas knew. O king! Having obtained the weapons and having been honoured, I lived there happily in Shakra’s abode, getting everything that I wished for.

—Yaksha Yudha Parva

Finally we see Chitrasena in the famous Ghosha yatra parva, where on the commands of Indra, he descends with his army to capture Kauravas for their insolent attitude. And his army had successfully routed Kaurava’s army, forcing Karna to flee and capture Kauravas along with their family.

It is at this point, at the behest of Yudhishthira, Pandavas tried to parley with Gandharvas to free Kauravas. However when they don’t buzz with Pandava’s ‘mild’ attitude, a fierce war broke out in which Chitrasena was defeated. Here is the final reference —

Dhananjaya used the weapon known as shabdabheda 58 and restrained his disappearance into many different forms. Pierced by the weapons of the great-souled Arjuna, the gandharva revealed himself to his beloved friend. 59 On seeing his friend Chitrasena, fighting feebly in battle, 60 the bull among the Pandavas withdrew the weapon he had released.

—Ghosha Yatra


ANGARAPARNA/CHITRARATHA/DAGHARATHA

Let me start by saying that we aren’t talking about three different person, rather one person in three stages of life. If I try to sequentialize the story based on references from Chitraratha Parva, it runs like this —

Once upon a time, there was a Gandharva named Chitraratha. He was famed by that named as such presumably because he owned a beautify decorated chariot. He was a ruler of a land near the banks of river Ganga known as Angaraparna and he assumed the same name for himself. It is at this place he encountered Arjuna as the family wandered after the death of Bakasura, before Draupadi’s swyamver event. The Gandharva introduced himself with these words —

Know me to be the gandharva named Angaraparna. I rely on my own strength. I am proud and jealous and I am Kubera’s beloved friend. This is my beautiful forest on the banks of the Ganga, known as Angaraparna. I dwell here. No corpses, 37 horned animals, gods or humans dare to set foot here. How dare you come?”

— Chitraratha Parva

We can check out this Gandharva was a friend of Kubera and not Indra. Here we also see the main bone of contention between Arjuna and Gandharvas. Gandharavas claimed the ownership of Ganga which Pandu objected. Arjuna debated that river Ganga is not bound by anyone’s ownership and Gandharvas had no right to bar anyone. At this Gandharva attacked Arjuna. However, Arjuna with Agneya weapon counter attacked, burning down his famed and beautiful chariot, knocking him unconscious and capturing him.

His wife appealed to Yudhishira for mercy and on the command of his elder brother Arjuna released the Gandharva from his captivity. This incident again shows the compassionate attitude of Pandavas towards the vanquished enemies, that has been a recurring theme through out the epic —

Which hero will kill an enemy who has been defeated in battle, has lost his fame and is now protected by a woman? Set him free.” Arjuna said, “O gandharva! Have your life. Go from here and do not grieve. Yudhishthira, king of the Kurus, 39 has ordered safety for you today.”

— Chitraratha Parva

At this point, the defeated Gandharva introduces himself with his original name and giving up the proud title he had assumed —

‘The gandharva said, “I have been defeated by you. Therefore, I will give up my earlier name of Angaraparna. Among men, I can no longer show my pride in strength or in name. I wanted to fight, with the powers of maya of the gandharvas, with someone who was at the peak of his youth. It is my good fortune that I encountered one with celestial weapons. My supreme and adorned chariot has been burnt by the agneya weapon. I was earlier called Chitraratha40 and have now become Dagdharatha. 41 I spoke to you about the knowledge that I earlier attained through austerities. Today, I will give it to the great-souled one who has granted me life. He who saves the life of a vanquished enemy who seeks sanctuary deserves good fortune.

There is an interesting and an important observation here. The term “Dagdharatha” means the burnt down chariot. That also indicates that the pride of the Gandharva is burnt down and now he takes on a name that would remind him of his defeat.

I would also wish the readers to take a special note of the last highlight. The grateful Gandharva acknowledges Pandava’s compassion and grants them special knowlddge. He acknowledges Pandava’s greatness of sparing the life of vanquished enemy, which was indeed rare then.

I wish we all learn and take away some useful lesson from this story.
