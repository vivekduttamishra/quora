Why did Gandhari blame Krishna for war instead of Shakuni, Duryodhana, and Dhratrashtra?

📅 Jun 18, 2020 03:39 AM PDT  |  🌐 English

Thanks for A2A

First part is simple question to answer — A common psychology. Let us ask another question first —

Why shouldn't Gandhari be blamed for the war?

Dhritrastra was blind, and ambitious. He blamed everyone for what he perceived as injustice. He could not see straight.

But what was the excuse of Gandhari?

  1. Why did she blindfolded herself rather than complementing her husband?
  2. Why was her blindfold more important than the upbringing of her children — Duryodhan was the product of blind ambition and blind arrogance. Can be blame him?
  3. Why didn't she blamed Duryodhan for Lakshagrih, Draupadi Cheerharan?
  4. Why didn't she persuaded his husband not to announce the war that resulted in the total devastation of her family?

Why did Gandhari blamed Krishna?

She knew Krishna, if wanted, could have prevented the war. And it was true. And thus she blamed. But the real reason she blamed him was —

  1. She wanted to shift her blame to someone else. It is much more easy to blame others than faulting ourselves.
  2. She didn't care about Dharma and Adharma, justice or injustice, lest she would have thanked Krishna for doing her job.
  3. She wanted her sons to live and prosper which indirectly mean let Pandavas languish as that was the only way her son's could have lived on.
  4. Her blame (and curse) to Krishna proves, she was no less ambitious and ruthless than her husband or sons, and a tad more hypocrite, to pass herself blameless.

Aside: Why Shakuni doesn't deserve to be blamed?

  1. Shakuni in contradiction to the modern narrative has a lesser role in Mahabharat — he is just the branch of evil tree.
  2. His single significant role was the planning and execution of the dice game. His role ended with Duryodhan's victory. He had no significant role in Cheerharan.
  3. He along with Vidur and Bhishma tried to persuade Duryodhana against the war. This is generally omitted in modern narratives.
