Who are the top 10 warriors in Dwapara Yuga?

📅 May 02, 2021 06:04 AM PDT  |  🌐 English

Thanks Soham for A2A. (Warning — Very long answer)

Before creating my list of warriors, I must clarify that, I believe there is a difference between these terms —

  • Warrior vs warmonger vs fighter — A warrior and a warmonger, both are fighters, but they have a difference. A warrior has ethics and would try to avoid a battle at all cost. He would fight for a cause greater than his ego. A war monger guided by his lust and ego would crave for a war. He would even reject the chance of avoiding a war.
  • Strong and Brave — Strong is a physical characteristic augmented by probably advanced weapons or numerically superior army. They may even win. But being strong or powerful is no guarantee of being brave. Brave is a trait of character. You need to have courage and perseverance that comes from moral or spiritual strengths.

So, when I would set down to make a list of top ten warriors, I would focus on warrior ethics rather than brute strength or skill. I would focus on courage rather than victory. You may not win and can still be brave and great warrior.

Take example of Maharana Pratap and Chandrashekhar Azad. They died for their motherland. They couldn’t win against numerically superior and powerful forces. But they were braver and more honorable than their adversary. I would call them greater warrior than Maan Singh, Akbar or Naut Babur (The SP who lead encounter of Chandrashekhar Azad).

Having set up the foundation of my answer, let me go to my answer. I would divide warriors in broad categories —


Top Warriors of Mahabharata

1.Arjuna

Only those who would chose to ignore all the epic evidence and ignore the words of Krishna, Parshurama, Bhishma, Drona, Karna and above all Mahadeva Shiva himself, would dispute this name at the top of the list. All the prominent warriors and fighters mentioned in the list expressed without any doubt, that Arjuna was the greatest warrior of the era.

If we go in details of what makes Arjuna the greatest warrior would make the answer really long, so I would delegate it to another post whose link, I would add here soon.

But to sum up —

  • Arjuna never ventured to a war out of his ego.
  • He applied only as much power as required to subdue his opponent.
  • He let his opponents withdrew gracefully after Draupadi’s Swyamavara battle, during Rajsuy Yagana, after Gandharva battle, and after Matasya battle. In each case he had his upper hand and could have humiliated his enemies. But he was Arjuna!
  • He was the only one to question the necessity of war and it was his question that gave the world one of its great treasures — Gita!
  • He constantly deployed mild forces against Bhishma, Drona and Kripacharya.
  • He fought Mahadeva himself and despite of the fact, that victory was impossible against Shiva, and the fact that he was badly mangled, he didn’t retreat. His perseverance pleased Mahadeva so much that not only he offered the greatest weapon of the universe to him, but personally trained him.
  • How many warriors can claim Shiva to be his guru?

For a detailed discussion on Arjuna refer to my upcoming post, whose link I would include here.

2. Bhishma

The veteran warrior, the ferocious Bhishma was a warrior of the highest order and has few unique feat that makes his career distinguished. Imagine a warrior who could defeat Parshurama! And can force Krishna to take up arms, against his own resolve! That was Bhishma.

Bhishma was the supreme protector of Hastinapur and was always in front to protect Hasitnapur. While the Digviajaya of Pandu is not detailed in the epic, it is imparative that Bhishma would have been a part of that expedition and there must have been a great reason that his reputation echoed throughout the epic.

Many may try to downplay Bhishma’s victory on Parshurama suggesting he had lost his divinity long before, but it doesn’t change the fact that Parshurama himself was the warrior of highest order and his name has been referred as one of the greatest warrior of all times in the epic itself. Every warrior carried high regard.

Bhishma, like a true warrior, tried to avoid the war as long as he could. Like a true general he knew the strength and weakness of his team and knew well how to settle the dispute in the midst of a battle. He was ethical enough to inform Kauravas before the start of the battle that he may not kill Pandavas who are deer to him.

Bhishma killed a limited number of solider per day — large enough to sap the opponent’s energy and not large enough to make him a war monger. He was the longest serving general of Kaurava’s army and he made opponent so desperate that Krishna himself had to break his vow!

Once Bhishma was gone the entire Kuru Army was scattered like a deck of cards.

3. Bhima

What makes Bhima a great warrior is not just the fact that he fulfilled his oath and destroyed all the hundred Kauravas just like he had announced, but that many of his battle were for the welfare of society and humanity — he had no reason to participate than to uphold Dharma.

He killed Bakasura to save an entire village from a cannibal. He defeated Jarasandha and freed a lot of king from his Prison. Few would go to the extent he went to make sure justice is delivered and was one of those Pandavas’s who didn’t change his view about Karna even after knowing his true identity.

4. Abhimanyu

Who can forget that great warrior who not only spoiled the best laid plan of Dronacharya to capture Yudhishthira but also exposed the true nature of all the Kaurava warriors — cowards.

When he fought he made warriors retreat as their loved ones were killed by a lone warrior — a typical picture of a young lion trapped by a heard of thousands of hyenas. The lion is alone and has slim chance of victory. But then he is lion and no hyena is willing to be near him alone. They are just too scared to approach him and trying to bite a piece of his flesh from behind.

The lion is killed. Lost his life. But won his battles. His opponents proved more powerful but Abhimanyu was more brave than all of them.

Abhimanyu’s death is a glaring example of how cowards can win the battle and a victory may have to hide its face in shame.


Now let us take a step down and discuss warrior that can’t be placed as same level as the above but still should be counted in —

5. Ashwatthama

Ashwatthama is an interesting case. He was one of the greatest fighters of the era with a host of divine weapons in his possession. What Ashwatthama lacked (and the reason of his part of Grade B) is character strength.

He wasn’t mentioned as part of any conspiracy of Duryodhana (contrary to depiction in Star Plus Mahabharata) and has often confronted team Duryodhana where then went wrong or aggresive. He challenged Karna when he used his abusive language against his gurus — Kripacharya and Dronacharya.

He also killed a large number of Pandavas forces including the son of Ghatotkatch.

He didn’t show the trait of a war monger till the point his father was killed by trick. And then we see a completely different Ashwatthama — brutal, ruthless and blinded by anger. He abused the divine weapons Narayanastra and Brahamstra in a feat of rage and that resulted in him getting cursed of eternal life.

He masacred all Up-pandavas getting inspired by an night predator and that heinous act became his only legacy.

But the symptom of his weak character is evident from the fact that he tried to trade his divyastra with Krishna’s Sudarshan Chakra.

6. Dronacharya

Dronacharya is another interesting case. He was undoubtedly one of the greatest teacher of weapons and battle of his time. He was a disciple of Parshurama, and received all his weapons as a legacy and he had the distinction of being the guru of the greatest warriors of the time. Yet Dronacharya wasn’t a warrior per-se.

There is no reference of any battle where Drona participated before Matasya battle where he was bested by his own protégée along with other Kuru warriors.

He became the the commander of the Kuru Army on eleventh day after Bhishma was ousted and was evidently not the first choice for the position. There wasn’t much that he achieved in the first two days (day 11 and 12) and refrained from forming Chakravyuh in presence of Arjuna.

When they managed to draw Arjuna away by tricks he though he would finally capture Yudhishthira, but Abhimanyu foiled their plan.

This time onward Drona’s leadership proved a disaster for both forces. While he killed thousands Pandavas soldiers including prominent generals like Drupada and Virata, he couldn’t prevent his troops from the wrath of Arjuna and Ghatotkatch.

Yet, Pandavas suffered maximum casualty by this father-son duo who wiped out — Abhimanyu, Drupada, Virata, Dhritdymna, Shikandi and all sons of Pandavas.

To credit Dorna, he tried his best to dissuade Duryodhana from entering into the battle.


The C Listers

This list doesn’t include warriors. It includes war-mongers and machineries who relishes fighting for lust, ego or money. They fought for egos, selfish goals and for recognitions. They never cared about humanity.

7. Karna

The name draw mix reaction. For many he is the greatest warrior for others, victim of an oppressive social structure and a tragic hero who was killed by unfair means. But make no mistake — the only part of this narrative that is true is the fact that his unfortunate mother had to abandon him while he was an infant — a misfortune for both mother and son. But story of misfortune ends there.

He was brought up by a caring and prosperous family and his foster father was a close friend of Prince Dhritrashtra of Hastinapur. And since he didn’t knew he was abandoned, it certainly couldn’t have been tormenting.

He was trained by best gurus of Aryavart and became a King at quite an young age and remained so for all his life. Karna became a prominent archer.

Karna form childhood befriended Duryodhana and both had one thing common — knack for shortcut to achieve their goal. Karna wanted Drona to him over a Brahmastra, so that he could be equal to Arjuna in battle. He was eyeing for a battle while still in gurukul!

Karna’s second encounter with Arjuna was probably the most successful. He demonstrated his skill by gatecrashing an event that was exclusively to show case Hastinapur’s princes’ skills. He was permitted to show case his valor. But that was not sufficient. He challenged Arjuna for a duel. Now what was the need of this uncalled for battle, other than satisfying his ego? That is the initial trait of a war monger.

Karna was part of that gang who wanted to throw a bride into fire because they failed to fulfil the terms of swyamvara. Karna jumped at Arjuna, fought vigorously and then withdrew scared because he thought his enemy is unvanquishable.

After the swyamvara, he provokes Duryodhana to fight and kill Pandavas before they grow stronger and get more allies — a war monger!

He was quick to second Susharma’s suggestion to attack and loot Matasya after Keechak was killed — that is a loot!

He advised Duryodhana to reject the peace offer by Krishna and himself rejected the Krishna plea to end the war. He requested Krishna to let the war happen knowning lacs of people would perish in this battle — that is a war monger!

But the worst was, when he announced in front of his mother that he would kill Arjuna, even after knowing that Arjuna was his own brother! Why, if not for his ego?

Karna during the battle vanquished Bhima on several occasion and even captured and let go Yudhishthira. But he didn’t kill Bhima who had taken an oath to kill all Kauravas. Some may say he did because he has taken an oath not to kill them. But they miss two points here —

  1. He was very abusive to the vanquished opponent, which is not a characteristic of a true warrior.
  2. He had taken no oath that he wouldn’t capture them. Capturing Yudhishthira would have meant end of battle. So why did he not capture Yudhishtira? Read this sentence again and you would understand the difference between a warrior and a war monger.

I didn’t mention how he humiliated Draupadi or how he persuaded Duryodhana to go to exile to mock the sufferings of Pandavas — those are the traits not fit even for a war-monger but a lowly human with a dirty mind!

8. Susharma and Trigarth Army

Can we call a pack of wild beasts a warrior? Trigarh was a state of machineries and looters. They attacked Matasya to loot them. When they failed they enlisted the help of like minded people — Duryodhana and Karna. In turn their they helped Kaurvas during Kurukshetra and Duryodhana used them for what they were — machineries. Seeing their expertise as warriors without honor, they were entrusted to form an army of samshaptakas. They weren’t supposed to win any battle but keep Arjuna away!


Honorable mentions

I am not ranking these warriors of Dwapara Yuga as they didn’t participate directly in the main epic war and are beyond any measure of ranking —

Krishna

Undoubtedly the greatest warrior and an incarnate of Vishnu. He had no comparision and he chose to sit out the war merely as advisor to Pandavas. He could have finished the war in a blink.

Parshurama

His name has been used in superlative terms, but he predates the Dwapara yuga and hence can’t be ranked in this era.

Balrama

The elder brother of Krishna was known for his powers. But he chose to abstain from Mahabharata. Had he participated no doubted it would have been from Pandava’s side as he could never go against his brother. He along with Krishna is a great example of restraint — two great warriors not fighting an epic war whose stories would echo for millenia!
