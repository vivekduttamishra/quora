Why are Ashwatthama and Ekalavya so unlucky? Why do both have to suffer so much?

📅 Jun 25, 2020 11:41 PM PDT  |  🌐 English

I am not sure, why people keep whining for anti-heros of Mahabharata and marking them unfortunate. Suffering makes people hero, those who run away from suffering become likes of Ashwatthama. Let us see how unlucky these people were —

Eklavya

  • No doubt lost his thumb. I will not discuss the reason here, that is evident from the life of Eklavya after the incident.
  • He became a King and remained a king till the end of his life.
  • He was an invited member in Yudhishthir’s Rajsuy yagna with a prominent role assigned.
  • What led to his death?
    • He partnered with Jarasandh, one of the prime evils (like Hitler) of his time.
    • He died by the hand of Krishna, for his crimes.
  • How was he unlukcy? He suffered for his mistakes.

Ashwstthama

  • Was born of a Brahmin and to remain poor and survive on alms was his true destiny like other Brahmin teachers of his time.
  • He became a King when Arjuna won Drupad and handed over to Drona.
  • Ungrateful, as he was, chose Duryodhana as his friend and fought against one person who made him King.
  • Forced his father to side with Duryodhana. I am not talking about the force they show it TV show. By aligning himself to Duryodhana he in a way psychologically force his father who loved him too much.
  • His alignment with Duryodhana was the prime reason of his father’s death.
  • Irrisponsibly used Naryanastra and Brahmastra.
  • Hunted sleeping warriors at the night like beasts.
  • Do you still find him unluky? Do you still think they suffered?

So let us see a few lucky guys whose lives were bed of roses —

  1. A couple
    1. They was thrown in prison and six of their sons were brutally murdered in front of their own eyes.
    2. They languished in prison for over 25 years under constant fear for the lives of their son.
  2. A King
    1. Who seven infant son were drowned to death and could do nothing against his wife who killed them.
    2. His deserving son gave up his personal life and crown for his happiness. Do you think, he was happy?
  3. A Prince
    1. Who gave up his crown for the happiness of his father.
    2. He saw the same thrown occupied by an ambitious blind man, and his sons and could do nothing because of the oath he had taken for his father.
    3. A dutiful son, that he was, died on a mesh of arrows, because his oath forced him to side with the evil son of the blind King sitting on the throne that was supposed to be his.
    4. He saw the entire kingdom devasted; he is responsible for the disaster. He failed his kingdom, not because he was evil, but because he was dutyful.
  4. A Rightful King and their family
    1. A rightful king gave up thrown because of a misktake he committed (not cirme).
    2. He lived his life in exile and died.
    3. He believed his brother, who succeeded him would take care of his family.
    4. His blind brother remained blind as his son’s tried to —
      1. Poison the sons of the King
      2. Tried to burn alive all his sons and wife
  5. The sons of the unfortunate King
    1. They were poisoned
    2. They were tried to be burnt alive
      1. They spent time in exile
    3. They were cheated and handed over a wasteland which they converted to a great empire.
    4. They were cheated again of the kingdom
    5. They were humiliated
    6. They were exiled for 13 years.

I find the definition of suffering and misfortune in this question heavily skewed.
