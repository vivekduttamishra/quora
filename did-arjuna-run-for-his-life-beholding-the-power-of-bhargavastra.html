Did Arjuna run for his life beholding the power of Bhargavastra?

📅 Apr 05, 2021 12:39 AM PDT  |  🌐 English

I Liked something that is said in another answer —

A truth is a truth even if no one believes it. A lie is a lie even if everyone believes it.

But then there are other equally significant quotes —

Lies have no legs to stand upon.

It needs the crutches of fabrication.

The truth is mentioned by Ved Vyas in no unclear terms and doesn’t matter even if no one wants to believe it —

  1. Bhargavastra was a powerful weapon. But there was a ethics involved in invocation of a Divyastra as stated by Shiva, Parshurama and Indra. You shouldn’t fire it on a weaker opponent and never unless you have other options. Truth is Karna violated the ethics of Divyastra.
  2. Arjuna was scared of Bhargavastra. There is no reference to say Bhargavastra was most powerful weapon. Shiva said Pashupata is the most powerful weapon. Krishna said the same. Karna himself said he had no weapon to compete with Arjuna’s higher weapons. So why didn’t Arjuna counter the weapon? Arjuna wasn’t sure what to do. He didn’t wanted to be reckless and deploy higher weapons against a weaker opponent, and couldn’t have countered it with lower weapons. It was as called catch-22 situation.
  3. Arjuna never fled — It was Krishna’s suggestion to move on (not flee) and let Karna exhaust himself. Now how was Karna be exhausted if no one fights him? Because Divyastra fired recklessly descends upon it’s intiator as mentioned by Indra himself. Ved Vyas never used the word fled.
  4. Truth is they left on a more important mission — ensure the safety of Yudhishthir, rather than wasting their time on a weapon that was to exhaust soon.
  5. Bhargavastra didn’t win the battle — The ultimate prove is the fact that Bhargavastra didn’t win the battle for Karna or Kauravas even when no one stopped it. It didn’t kill any Pandava or Panchala or Vrishni or any Maharatha.
  6. Bhargavastra was powerful, Karna wasn’t. Karna’s reckless use of Bhargavastra against foot soliders rendered it ineffective.

One simple way to prove a lie is a lie is to try proving it correct and fail doing so. This is a simple and well established principle — proof by contradiction.

If Bhargavastra fired by Karna was as power as it is claimed and if Arjuna really fled out of fear then —

  • Didn’t Bhargavastra slauther entire Pandava’s Army? No
  • Was it Arjuna’s idea to move? NO.
  • Did Ved Vyas use the word flee in this case? NO.
  • Didn’t it kill any warrior of repute? No.
  • Did it win the war for Kauravas? No.
  • Was it diffused without killing Arjuna or any other prominent warrior? YES.
  • Who diffused it?
  • Did Krishna say Karna would be exhausted? YES
  • Why would Karna get exhausted when everyone is afraid to fight him and no one does?
  • Is there any mention of Divyastra acting against its wearer if he is reckless in it’s use? YES.

Then there is a difference between bravery and foolishness —

  • There was no point in countering a weapon recklessly fired that would exhaust his own initor.
  • Had Arjuna fought the weapon, it would have given it a valid target. And that would be stupid.

Yes, I think I agree — truth is a truth, even if you don’t want to believe and lies don’t have legs.
