How does the battle of Kurukshetra operate allegorically? What does the war represent beyond simply the war between the Kauravas and Pandavas? Which lines from the Gita suggest that the war of Kurukshetra may not be a literal war at all?

📅 Sep 18, 2021 07:13 AM PDT  |  🌐 English

Thanks for the A2A.

Every incident, every event in our life can be perceived allegorically in retrospection. Human mind is great in finding symbolism and what is the lasting value of something if we can find a lesson from it for the future?

I am aware of a school of thought which assumes that the entire episode was just a symbolism with five Pandavas representing virtues and hundred Kauaravas similarly representing vices and so on. And one psychological reason for propagation of this perspective is our inability to come to the terms with the fact that all compassionate God may advocate violence or war, and it appears a safe heaven to believe it metaphorically.

That said, it would be a capital mistake to assume that it wasn’t a literal war at all. Not only symbolism is no substitute for fact, it also undermines the underlying lesson of the epic and Gita where this question seeks to find contradiction.

Assuming that it wasn’t really a literal war would devalue all the very real concerns and pains of Arjuna. If there was no pain, no real struggle between duty and selfish interest, Krishna’s guidelines would reduce to a mere pep talk. But it doesn’t stop even here, and gets even worse.

Assuming Gita discourse as merely a symbolism, which it wasn’t, It would mean Krishna didn’t really persuaded Arjuna towards the war. But the war and the dilemma that Arjuna faced are as real today as they were all those thousands of years ago — Why should I put mine or other’s lives at risk? For just a piece of land? Is land more worth than all those lives? Now ask yourself, how would you persuade your army to fight, if you are going to condemn violence. How are you going to defend your nation? One way could be to assume that these wars too are just allegory. Alas that’s not how the world works.

Krishna however doesn’t condone violence. He doesn’t call violence as sin. He condemns inaction or affliction to fruits that can dissuade you from your duty. He proclaims that even if what people may consider sin, if done in the line of selfless (without attachment) duty, it should be considered as a righteous deed as explained in Gita 4.36 and 5.10.

Gita’s knowledge is profound because it is based on real world and not just some untested lofty symbolism.

Short Summary

Mahabharata war and its narratives beyond doubt is based on real world incident. However, the lessons of Gita and Mahabarata can also be taken metamorphically as is true with any other tale of the world, real of fictional.
