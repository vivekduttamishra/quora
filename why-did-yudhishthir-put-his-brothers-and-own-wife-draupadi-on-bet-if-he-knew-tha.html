Why did Yudhishthir put his brothers and own wife, Draupadi on bet? If he knew that they were cheated, why didn't he get out of the game?

📅 Jun 06, 2021 06:35 AM PDT  |  🌐 English

Thanks for the A2A.

What Yudhishthir did was a blunder and a conclusive proof of decline of social order. It would be more apparent by the fact that almost nobody could declare his act as wrong!

The problem was the fact that even a learned person like Yudhishthira couldn’t differentiate between Dharma and Paramapara. When invited for a gamble, it was a tradition to accept such invitation. But Yudhisthira mistook it as his Dharma. And again the fact that no one could point that out shows that this mistake wasn’t an individual mistake but one that was practices and accepted by the entire society.

Once the gambling started, Yudhishthira’s option diminished. We must realize that there was no court of arbitration at that point of time. King’s words were the law and accepted agreement. If a King backs down his own words, who would trust him? If declining an invitation was difficult, getting out was now impossible. Yudhishthira had to see through the end of the game.

It wasn’t easy to become like Krishna — a ranchod! It took years of maturity for Arjuna to reach that stage when he questioned the need of a war. Such conciousness needed to become the new social order.

While accusing Yudhishthira for putting Draupadi as a bait, which is a valid accusation, we often miss out one important point — He baited himself before baiting Draupadi. And if we see the arguments of Karna, it would be apparent that no matter Yudhishthira had baited her or not, she would have suffered the safe fate in the hand of those deceitful creatures. In that sense, Draupadi was like a straw for a drowning man — she was more like their last hope to reclaim everyone’s freedom including Draupadi’s.

None of these arguments intends to applaud Yudhishthir’s action. He followed traditions blindly, read shastra’s literally rather than grasping their essence and created a terrible precedence — something that was censured then and is censured now. He also suffered the consequences of his foolishness. He suffered 13 years of exile. He repented and became a better person than before. That I think, is one of the morals of the epic.
