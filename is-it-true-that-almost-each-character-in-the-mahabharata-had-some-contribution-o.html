Is it true that almost each character in the Mahabharata had some contribution (or faults) which ultimately led to the battle?

📅 Apr 27, 2020 08:41 PM PDT  |  🌐 English

Thanks for the A2A.

This is a wonderful question.

Yes, many a character's action directly or indirectly led to ultimate war — including even the heros. Although we can't generalize this statement to every single character, that's beyond the point.

This is what make Mahabharat more real, relatable, and infinitely more valuable. This is Karma theory.

  1. Every action has consequences, and consequences are often beyond our control. Many acted thinking they were too powerful to be brought to justice. We know the consequences.
  2. Even an action with pure intention may be disastrous if you plan to control the destiny, you are condemned — Bhishma
  3. Your wisdom can't protect you from the fruit of your actions — Drona.
  4. Actions based blind trust on tradition and protocol, and misinterpreting Dharma will surely lead you to disaster — Yudhisthir.
  5. Even inaction is an action — Pandava brothers’ inaction during chirharan is a glaring example.

Most Important — Krishna!

If a powerful person, who is capable of anything, initiates a peace treaty and fails, should he be blamed? Of course he should be. But that rises to another question that in my understanding settles and unsettles everything, all at once —

  1. Did Krishna, really wanted to prevent the war
    1. How did he fail in a peace talk, not once but twice? Both Duryodhana and Karna refused his advise.
    2. Why did he convinced a reluctant arjun to fight taking him through Gita lessons of Dharma, Karma, and devotion?
  2. Was the battle really bad and avoiding it was best course of action?
    1. Answer lies in the previous question. Think again!
