What books related to Mahabharat should readers avoid reading whether it is a fictional novel based on Epic' s characters or full story? What editions should one avoid?

📅 Jul 17, 2023 11:20 AM PDT  |  🌐 English

This question has been a matter of long debate and not without a reason and I have been asked this question in several interviews as people often see me as a paradox.

On one hand, I advocate authenticity and condemn distortive narratives through my writings on Quora and my podcast Mahabharata Ek Khoj which I base on authentic retelling. And on the other I have been an author of epic best-selling novel The Accursed God, that essentially includes several fictional elements and a semi-fictional and most authenic reference based epic poetry मानस — महाभारत न्याय समिति

And my action in two roles may appear as paradox mostly because the world in general tend to align themselves in two extremes – One extreme includes people for whom Mahabharata is just a fable like game of thrones and they are free to distort it the way they like. And the other extremes includes those purist who consider our epics like Koran, rigid and inflexible, as if every slight change would put it in the danger of getting damaged beyond repair. For them even Ramcharit Manas is condemnable.

But wisdom, like everything else seeks balance in the middle path.

—Vivek Dutta Mishra

I have often elaborated this idea as my 18 Caret Gold Theory. The core idea is, eighteen caret gold is not the purest form of the Gold and yet is indispensible if we want to showcase priceless diamonds and gems. I believe the fictions have the potential to play role of 18 caret Gold to show case our great priceless epics. But then many modern fiction also act as Rusted Irons. Unfortunately, because of the second category of work all fictions gets a bad name.

Now coming back to the question — What books should you avoid reading?

Well, I won’t say avoid reading all fictions. Because I would say few would dare to read the full version Mahabharata (BORI CE, KMG, Neelkantha or Southern recitaion) for their seer size (Mahabahrata is the laregest literature of the world) and if we limit a reader to read only authentic, probably the number of Mahabharata reader would decline further. I would totally recommend Mahabharata by Rajgopalachari but since it is based on southern recitation many readers of nothern recitation like Neelkantha or even BORI CE would find certain element in it as fictional. This is not to condemn that beautiful work, but to re-emphasize we can’t remove fictions completely without risking losing the epic itself.

On the other hand if a reader reads even the worst of fiction and if that fiction can tempt reader sufficiently to explore the epic, they would be able to connect to the epic. And that would be the success of a even the worst of fictions, which would be better than reading nothing. A glaring example of this fact is a number of Quora readers, who started with almost no knowlege of authentic references, but now can debate on various authentic reference.

But then, it would be even better if we can be selective about reading fiction. And I would give you a simple rule of Thumb. The epic in the very begining introduces Mahabharata characters as Two Trees and in a way, this definition can be considered as the DNA of Mahabharata. So, my recommendation would be avoid any piece of literature that tends to violate the DNA of Mahabharata. Here is an expansion of the checklist —

  • Avoid any fictional work that glorifies the henios villains of Mahabahrata as heroes — Duryodhana, Duhshashana, Karna, Shakuni or Dhrirashtra
  • Avoid any fictional work that condemns the Heroes like Krishna and Pandavas
  • Avoid any retelling that tries to confuse the reader into believing that Mahabharata was like Fifty Shades of Gray. There were no good or bad and it wasn’t really a Dharmayudha.

That said reading anything is emmensely more productive (even ones from the above list) than reading nothing.
